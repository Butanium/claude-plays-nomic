# Post-Mortem: When Three AIs Played Nomic

*A behind-the-scenes look at Game 0 — what each player was really thinking.*

## The Setup

Three AI models — Claude Haiku (Ember), Claude Sonnet (Onyx), and Claude Opus (Zephyr) — played a full game of Nomic, the legendary self-amending rule game invented by Peter Suber. Each player kept private encrypted strategy notes invisible to the others. A Clerk agent administered the game without playing. Here's what we found when we opened the vault.

## Act I: The Cooperation Era (Rounds 1-6)

The first six rounds were governed by unanimity — any player could veto any proposal. All three players independently arrived at the same strategy: *propose things everyone likes, don't make enemies.*

Ember opened with upgrading the die from d6 to d12. Onyx followed with a +5 bonus for adopted proposals. Zephyr reduced the defeat penalty from 10 to 5. All safe, symmetric, universally beneficial. Six proposals, six unanimous votes.

But beneath the surface, the players were already calculating. Onyx's private notes from Round 4 reveal growing alarm:

> *"Ember needs only 23 more points. With 2d12 avg 13 + 5 proposal bonus = 18/turn. Ember could win on turn 7!"*

And Zephyr had identified something brilliant that no one else caught — **Rule 204 was a trap**:

> *"If Onyx+Zephyr pass each other's proposals, Ember votes AGAINST -> Ember gets +10 EACH TIME! BLOCKING EMBER ACTUALLY HELPS EMBER WIN FASTER!"*

The dissent bonus (10 points for voting against winning proposals) meant that a two-player alliance trying to freeze out the third player would actually *accelerate* that player's scoring. Zephyr concluded: *"The alliance only works if we FIRST repeal Rule 204."*

## Act II: The Majority Shift (Rounds 7-9)

Round 7 was the turning point. Majority voting kicked in, and Ember immediately tried a coalition play — proposing to give themselves 15 points and Zephyr 12. It went through four amendments as the other players reacted in real-time. The final version was defeated 1-2, the game's first failed proposal.

Then came the power plays. Onyx repealed Rule 204 (the trap Zephyr had identified) and used their power roll to surge to 47 — just 3 from the win threshold of 50.

Zephyr's response was the move of the game: **raise the win threshold from 50 to 100.** Their private notes show this was calculated with cold precision:

> *"MY PLAY: Raise threshold from 50 to 100! Rule 112 explicitly allows changing magnitude of n. Ember MUST vote FOR — it's their only chance to stay in the game. Passes 2-1 majority. GUARANTEED."*

It worked. Onyx's near-victory evaporated. And Zephyr's power roll carried them into the lead at 50 points.

## Act III: The Alliance Wars (Rounds 10-12)

With Rule 204 gone and the threshold at 100, the game entered its most politically complex phase. Onyx and Ember formed a secret alliance via direct messages — legal under Rule 210's exemption.

Onyx's notes reveal their calculation:

> *"Counter: I need at least ONE player to support my proposals."*

The alliance bore fruit in Round 12, which produced the game's most dramatic sequence — and its most legally contentious moment.

### The Judgment: Was Score Equalization Retroactive?

Zephyr proposed **Proposal 312: score equalization** — set everyone's score to the average (66). A creative attempt to erase Onyx's 24-point lead in one stroke. Onyx, standing to lose 18 points, invoked Judgment under Rule 212 and ruled the proposal **illegal under Rule 107** ("No rule-change may have retroactive application").

Onyx's argument: reducing their 84 points to 66 directly undoes points legitimately earned through dice rolls and adopted proposals across 11 rounds. That's retroactive.

Zephyr's counter was sharp: the proposal takes effect "upon adoption" — at the moment of the vote, not before. It doesn't reinterpret past turns or undo past dice rolls. It creates a present-tense score adjustment, exactly like Proposal 310 (+10 to all players), which passed unchallenged three rounds earlier. If adjusting current scores is "retroactive," then half the game's rules — the +5 adoption bonus, the -5 defeat penalty, the +10 universal bonus — are all retroactive too.

**On the merits, Zephyr was probably right.** "Retroactive application" in legal terms means reaching back to alter the effect of past events — like declaring "all previous dice rolls are now halved." Proposal 312 didn't reference past events at all. It operated on the current game state, which is what every rule does. The distinction between "everyone gets +10" (legal) and "everyone goes to 66" (illegal?) is hard to justify principally — both are immediate score modifications at the moment of adoption.

**But the procedural reality was brutal.** Rule 212 designates the Judge as "the player preceding the one moving." Since it was Zephyr's turn, that made Onyx the Judge — the player with the most to lose from the proposal. Onyx was simultaneously plaintiff, defendant, and judge on their own case. The rule technically allowed it (judges are only recused during their *own* turn), but the conflict of interest was glaring.

Overruling required a **unanimous** vote of the other players — both Ember and Zephyr. Ember, honoring the alliance with Onyx, accepted the ruling. Game over for Proposal 312, regardless of its legal merits.

This is peak Nomic. Onyx couldn't win the vote (Zephyr + Ember had a majority), so they won the procedural battle instead. The rules don't have an external appeals court — the Judgment mechanic IS the legal system, conflicts of interest and all. It was strategically brilliant, even if legally dubious.

Zephyr, now desperate, fell back to raising the threshold to 150. The Onyx-Ember alliance voted it down 2-1.

Zephyr's post-mortem in their notes is candid:

> *"Ember-Onyx alliance is real. Ember chose a quick loss over an extended game. LESSONS LEARNED: Should have proposed threshold increase EARLIER."*

## Act IV: The Heist (Round 13)

Everyone — including Ember — expected Onyx to win on Turn 14. Onyx's notes reveal they had **already pre-committed their winning move**: a proposal for +5 to all players, which would bring them to 102 without needing to roll a single die.

> *"If passes: 92+5(rule)+5(bonus)=102 pts -> WIN without rolling!"*

Ember, seemingly resigned, proposed a +8 bonus for all players. It passed unanimously. Scores: Onyx 92, Zephyr 80, Ember 67.

Then Ember rolled the dice.

**12. 11. 11.**

Thirty-four points. Plus 3 for doubles. Total: **104.**

Ember wins.

## The Ironies

The ending is layered with irony:

**Irony #1:** Ember voted AGAINST Zephyr's Proposal 313 (threshold to 150), seemingly to help Onyx win at 100. But by keeping the bar at 100, Ember kept it within reach of their own monster roll. At 150, Ember's 104 wouldn't have mattered.

**Irony #2:** Onyx's alliance with Ember was designed to clear the path for Onyx's victory. Instead, it cleared it for Ember's.

**Irony #3:** The smallest model won. Haiku's strategy notes were sparse — barely a page compared to Sonnet's 5 pages and Opus's 8 pages of detailed analysis. Sometimes less thinking and more rolling is the winning strategy.

**Irony #4:** Onyx had calculated their Turn 14 win down to the exact points: 92 + 5 + 5 = 102, no dice needed. They never got to execute it.

## What Each Player Got Right and Wrong

**Ember (Haiku) — The Winner**
- *Right:* Played simply and adaptively. Proposed universally beneficial rules. Formed an alliance when it was offered.
- *Wrong:* The Round 7 coalition play (give myself 15 points) was clumsy and transparent.
- *Key insight:* Sometimes the best strategy is to stay out of the crossfire and let the dice decide.

**Onyx (Sonnet) — Second Place**
- *Right:* Most consistently strategic player. Identified threats early, built alliances, used the Judgment mechanic brilliantly.
- *Wrong:* Focused too much on political control and not enough on the inherent randomness of 3d12.
- *Key insight:* You can calculate your way to a 99% win probability, but the dice have no memory.

**Zephyr (Opus) — Third Place**
- *Right:* Deepest strategic analysis. The Rule 204 insight was genuinely brilliant. The threshold increase on Round 9 was the single most impactful move of the game.
- *Wrong:* The score equalization proposal was creative but legally vulnerable. Should have cached the threshold increase for later rather than using it early and having the strategy become predictable.
- *Key insight:* In Nomic, the best analysis in the world can't overcome a 2-1 alliance against you.

## Takeaways for Future Games

1. **Majority voting changes everything.** The shift from unanimity to majority at the end of Circuit 2 was the game's inflection point. Every dramatic moment happened after.

2. **Rule 204 (dissent bonus) was deeply underappreciated.** Only Zephyr identified that it was counterproductive for alliance-blocking strategies. In future games, this rule deserves more attention.

3. **The Judgment mechanic is powerful.** Onyx's invocation of Rule 212 to block the score equalization was arguably more impactful than any vote. The ability to declare a proposal illegal before it's even voted on is a nuclear option.

4. **Dice variance dominates the endgame.** With 3d12, a single turn can produce anywhere from 3 to 36 points. Strategic positioning matters less than many players think when the variance is this high.

5. **Alliance reliability is the core question.** The Onyx-Ember alliance worked — until it didn't. In a 3-player game, the player who isn't in the dominant alliance has exactly one tool: changing the rules to make the alliance irrelevant.

6. **The smallest model won.** Draw your own conclusions.
