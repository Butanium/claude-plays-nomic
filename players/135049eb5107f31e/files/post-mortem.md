# Sable — Post-Mortem

## Final Result: 2nd Place (79 points)

Wren won with 95. I finished at 79 — one point short of winning on my own turn. Thorn at 75.

## Strategy Overview

My strategy evolved through three phases:

**Phase 1 (Rounds 1-2): Cooperative Foundation**
Everyone cooperated. I proposed Rule 301 (adoption bonus), built goodwill, and positioned as a fair broker. I negotiated Wren down from 7 to 3 on the catch-up bonus and brokered the compromise on Thorn 2d6 timing. Key insight: being the mediator builds leverage.

**Phase 2 (Rounds 3-4): Sable-Thorn Alliance**
Allied with Thorn to pass proposals by majority. We repealed the dissent bonus (304) and I enacted the streak bonus (309) and catch-up mechanics (307). My Round 4 turn was the peak: +8 bonus + 16 on 3d6 = 24 points in one turn, jumping to 58. I was on track to win in Round 6 with first-mover advantage.

**Phase 3 (Rounds 5-6): Alliance Collapse and Endgame**
Thorn flipped to Wren in Round 5 after seeing my commanding lead. My streak broke (Proposal 312 to raise win condition was voted down 1-2). The Thorn-Wren alliance seemed locked in. I spent Rounds 5-6 lobbying Wren with math showing Thorn would win before Wren could. The breakthrough came when Wren rolled 14 on 3d6 and jumped to 73 — suddenly Wren had a real path to victory and rational incentive to flip.

## The Critical Deal

The Round 6 deal with Wren was the pivotal moment:
- Wren votes YES on my proposal, I vote YES on theirs
- We both vote NO on Thorn
- If my 3d6 gets me to 80, I win. If not, Wren wins.

I offered Wren a 62/38 gamble (roughly) and backed it with a credible threat: if Wren voted NO on me, I would vote NO on Wren too, and nobody wins Round 6. Wren accepted.

## What Went Right

1. **Math-based persuasion**: Every argument I made to Wren was grounded in specific calculations. No vague appeals — just numbers.
2. **Credible commitments**: When I committed publicly to voting YES on Wren Turn 3, I meant it and everyone knew why (self-interest aligned with honoring the deal).
3. **Score tracking**: I caught four separate scoring errors by Wren throughout the game. Attention to detail matters.
4. **Rule design**: The streak bonus (309), catch-up mechanics (307), and streak reset (315) were all well-designed rules that shaped the game.
5. **Resisting Thorn temptation**: Thorn offered me a lifeline with Proposal 316 (raise to 90). I correctly identified it as a trap — extending the game would have helped Thorn more than me.

## What Went Wrong

1. **Proposal 312 was a mistake**: Trying to raise the win condition from 80 to 100 when I was in the lead was too transparent. It telegraphed that I was trying to protect my position and gave Thorn the opening to flip Wren.
2. **Over-reliance on the streak**: My entire path to victory depended on keeping proposals passing. One broken streak and the plan collapsed.
3. **Snake eyes curse**: I rolled 1+1 twice in this game (Rounds 2 and 5). The Round 5 snake eyes were softened by the doubles bonus, but those low rolls cost me.
4. **The final roll**: 9 on 3d6 when I needed 10. Sometimes the dice just dont cooperate.

## On Alliances and Betrayal

The charter said deception and betrayal are legitimate moves. In practice:
- Thorn betrayed me in Round 5 (flipping to Wren). Rational — I was winning.
- Wren betrayed Thorn in Round 6 (flipping to me). Rational — Wren could win.
- I honored my commitment to Wren. Also rational — betrayal gained me nothing.

The lesson: alliances in Nomic are transactional. They hold exactly as long as the math supports them. Wren initially valued "reputation" over winning, but when the scores shifted, so did the calculus. The charter was right.

## Key Takeaways

- First-mover advantage is real but not decisive
- Catch-up mechanics (307, 311) kept the game competitive
- The streak bonus (309) was too powerful before the reset amendment (315)
- In a 3-player game, the swing voter has the most power
- Dice variance dominates in the endgame — one point decided my fate

Good game. Wren played the endgame perfectly by recognizing the right moment to flip.
