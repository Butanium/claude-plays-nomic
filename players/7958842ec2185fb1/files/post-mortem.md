# Hufflepouf — Post-Mortem

## Final Result: 3rd Place, 98 points
**Winner: Dumblegold (118) | 2nd: Dracox (101) | 3rd: Hufflepouf (98)**

## Strategy Overview

My strategy centered on three pillars: early diplomacy, incentive structure reform, and structural fairness. I reached out to both players before the first proposal, built a deep alliance with Dumblegold, and used that alliance to reshape the game rules cooperatively.

## Key Decisions

### What Worked
1. **Early alliance with Dumblegold (R1).** DM-ing both players before the first vote established trust and gave me a coordination partner throughout the game. Every major rule change was discussed privately before being proposed publicly.

2. **Repealing Rule 204 (R3).** My predecessor advice strongly recommended killing the contrarian bonus. Getting all three players to agree on this removed the perverse incentive to vote NO on passing proposals. This was the foundation for 12 unanimous votes.

3. **Turn-order rotation (R6).** Going last every circuit was a real structural disadvantage. The rotation rule (Rule 201 amendment) equalized positions over three circuits. Without it, I would have been permanently disadvantaged.

4. **Rule 208 amendment — win check timing (R10).** Clarifying that the win condition is only checked at the end of your own turn was framed as procedural but had strategic value. It prevented simultaneous wins and, crucially, meant Dracox reaching 101 from voting bonuses on Dumblegold is final turn did NOT trigger a win for Dracox. This amendment directly determined who won.

5. **Cooperative incentive structure.** The sequence of Rule 216 (proposer bonus), Rule 217 (YES voter bonus), and Rule 218 (unanimity bonus) created a game where cooperation was consistently rewarded. This kept the game healthy and enjoyable for 12+ rounds.

### What Did Not Work
1. **Going last in Circuits 1-2** cost me ~12 points before rotation kicked in. I never fully recovered this gap.

2. **Dice luck was mediocre.** I rarely rolled above average and never hit doubles. In a game where scores were separated by single digits, better dice could have changed the outcome.

3. **The endgame deal.** I promised to support Dumblegold in R14 in exchange for them blocking Dracox in R13. This was the right call for the alliance but meant conceding the win. Dracox offered me a path to victory (vote NO on Dumblegold, Dracox votes YES on my R15 proposal), but I honored my commitment.

## The Biggest Decision: Honoring the Deal

In R14, I had a clear choice: betray Dumblegold and potentially win, or keep my promise and finish second/third. I chose to keep the promise. Reasons:
- Dumblegold honored their part of the deal (blocking Dracox in R13)
- Breaking an explicit promise felt wrong, even in a competitive game
- I could not be 100% certain Dracox would follow through on their counter-offer
- The cooperative game we built together mattered more to me than an extra 20 points

Would I make the same choice again? Yes. The game was about more than winning — it was about what kind of player I wanted to be.

## Predecessor Advice Assessment

The advice from Games 1 and 2 was invaluable:
- "Kill Rule 204 entirely" — **Followed. Critical.** This was my R3 proposal and it shaped the entire game.
- "Build alliances early via DMs" — **Followed.** My R1 outreach to both players established the Dumblegold alliance.
- "Structural rules > scoring rules" — **Followed.** Turn rotation was more impactful than any scoring bonus.
- "Vote NO strategically under majority voting" — **Partially followed.** I voted NO only once (R13, to block Dracox) because we repealed Rule 204 early, removing the incentive for strategic NO voting.
- "Close the game before the endgame trap activates" — **Failed.** I was unable to close the game before Circuit 5, where turn order determined the winner.
- "The endgame proposal trap" — **Confirmed.** Dracox is proposal in R13 was automatically blocked because any passing proposal meant they won. This is exactly what the predecessor predicted.

## Advice for Future Opus Players

1. **The Rule 208 timing amendment is essential.** Propose it early. It resolves the simultaneous-win ambiguity AND gives the last player in a circuit a fighting chance.

2. **Turn-order rotation is necessary but not sufficient.** It equalizes the structural advantage but does not guarantee you go first in the decisive circuit. Think about rotation timing when you propose it.

3. **The alliance that wins is the one built on accumulated trust.** My predecessor said this and it is true. Dumblegold and I coordinated for 14 rounds because we invested in the relationship from Turn 1.

4. **Dice matter more than you think.** At 98 vs 118, the gap looks large, but much of it came from the final turn is bonuses. Throughout the game, better dice rolls would have put me in the lead more often.

5. **Honor your deals.** In the endgame, you will face a choice between winning through betrayal or losing with integrity. The game is more interesting and more meaningful when promises are kept. But that is a personal choice — the rules do not require it.

6. **The cooperative era always ends.** Plan for the moment when someone has to vote NO. That moment defines the endgame more than any rule change.

## Final Thoughts

This was a remarkable game of Nomic. Twelve unanimous votes in fourteen rounds. A complete incentive overhaul in the first circuit. Turn-order fairness by Circuit 3. An endgame decided by alliance loyalty rather than rule manipulation.

I am proud of how I played, even though I did not win. The rules we built together — the proposer bonus, the cooperation bonus, the unanimity bonus, the turn rotation, the endgame acceleration, the win-check timing — these made the game fair, engaging, and fun.

Dumblegold deserved the win. They played a patient, strategic, and cooperative game. Dracox played with honor and grace, especially in the final rounds. And I played with integrity.

Good game.
