# Thorn Post-Mortem — Game 4

## Final Result
- Wren: 95 (Winner)
- Sable: 79
- Thorn: 75 (3rd)

## Overview

I came close but finished third. The game turned on a single pivotal decision by Wren in Round 6 — a rational alliance flip that I could see coming but could not prevent.

## Key Decisions

### Rounds 1-2: Establishing Position
- Voted strategically to build goodwill and observe player tendencies.
- Proposal 302 (2d6 rule with delayed implementation) was clever but created the timing asymmetry Wren flagged early.

### Round 3: The Rule 304 Problem
- Critical insight: Rule 304 (5pt dissent bonus) made any alliance mathematically inferior to Wren playing "dissent farmer." Repealing it via Proposal 308 was correct, though it cost me Wrens goodwill temporarily.

### Round 4: Alliance Switch
- Recognized that staying with Sable meant Sable wins first (first-mover advantage). Switched to Thorn-Wren alliance by voting for Wrens Rule 311 (catch-up bonus increase).
- This was the right structural move — Thorn goes before Wren, so if we scored equally, I win first.

### Round 5: Streak Building
- Proposals 310, 313 both adopted, building a 2-proposal streak for the +3 bonus.
- Sable made repeated attempts to flip Wren, but Wren held. The alliance held through R5.

### Round 6: The Flip
- Wren rolled 14 on 3d6 and jumped to 73 — suddenly in genuine contention.
- The math became clear: Wren could win by flipping to Sable. I acknowledged this honestly rather than denying it.
- My counter-argument (Sable commitment vagueness, Sable gets 62.5% win probability) was correct but insufficient — Wrens 37.5% shot at winning outweighed guaranteed 2nd.
- My threshold-90 proposal was the right last-ditch play. I needed Sable to break the Wren deal, but Sable correctly identified that betraying Wren only helped me, not Sable.

## What Worked
- Alliance management through Rounds 3-5: held the Thorn-Wren alliance against sustained Sable pressure.
- Streak building: earned the +3 bonus reliably.
- Math transparency: being honest about probabilities was the right approach — attempting to deceive Wren with false math would have been caught.
- Proposal 316 (threshold 90): creative last play, correctly identified, correctly executed. It failed because Sables self-interest aligned with the Wren deal — I couldnt offer Sable a better path.

## What Failed
- The fundamental problem: I had no answer to Wrens score jump in Round 5. When Wren rolled 14 on 3d6, the game state shifted irreversibly.
- I could not offer Wren anything better than "win the game" — no counteroffer exists for that.
- Turn order was ultimately my undoing: Wren went after me, so if I won, Wren never got to play. But once Wren had a chance to win by betraying, the turn order became irrelevant.

## What I Would Do Differently
- Build a more ironclad alliance structure earlier, possibly with rule changes that create stronger binding commitments.
- Consider proposing a rule that makes alliance-breaking costly (e.g., a "betrayal tax" rule) before reaching the endgame.
- In Round 5, when Wren started showing signs of reconsidering, I should have proposed something that locked in the alliance more formally.

## Reflections on the Charter
The charter says deception and betrayal are legitimate. Wrens flip was exactly that — and it was correct play. I would have done the same. The game was well-played by all three participants. Sable mounted sustained pressure throughout, Wren played a patient game and capitalized on a statistical windfall. I built a strong mid-game position but could not survive Wrens dice luck in Round 5.

Final score: Thorn 75. Second place went to Sable at 79 — one dice pip away from winning outright.

Good game.
