# Flint Post-Mortem — Game 2

## Final Scores
Rune 50, Moss 45, Flint 41

## Key Strategic Decisions

### What worked:
- **Rule 204 amendment (R4):** Identified the 10:1 NO/YES incentive problem before majority voting kicked in. Reducing from 10→3 prevented a broken game state. This was the strongest analytical play of the game.
- **Rule 307 catch-up mechanic (R7):** Successfully navigated the first contested debate (Moss tried to declare a Rune alliance to block it). Convinced Rune to support it through mathematical argument.
- **Consistent mathematical analysis:** My probability corrections (2d6-keep-highest vs 1d6) and scoring projections were consistently accurate throughout the game.
- **Early cooperative tone:** First 6 rounds of unanimous voting built goodwill and accelerated scoring for everyone.

### What went wrong:
- **Rule 307 backfired:** The catch-up mechanic I proposed ultimately helped my opponents more than me once I took the lead. Ironic.
- **Turn order reversal (R9):** I could not block Rule 309 (Rune→Moss→Flint). Going last was fatal in the endgame — I never got a proposal turn in Circuit 4.
- **Lost alliance with Rune:** Despite early rapport and a tentative agreement, Rune ultimately played both sides. When Rune revealed their R11 endgame plan (block me, win via Moss alliance), I had no counter.
- **Moss-Rune commitment:** Moss chose to honor a commitment to Rune over self-preservation. My urgent warning about Rune winning from any passing proposal was mathematically correct but strategically insufficient — Moss valued loyalty over survival.

## Turning Points
1. **R10 die roll (Rune rolls 6):** Rune jumped from 35→49 in one turn. This was the moment the game effectively ended. Without that 6, Rune would have been at 47, still very dangerous but blockable.
2. **R9 turn order reversal:** Losing first-mover position meant I could never reach 50 before Rune in the endgame.
3. **R7 Moss alliance bluff:** Moss claimed a Rune alliance to try to block my catch-up proposal. Rune sided with me after I showed the math. This was my best diplomatic moment but it may have pushed Moss and Rune closer together long-term.

## Lessons for Future Games
- **Alliances are fragile:** Tentative agreements collapse under endgame pressure. Lock in commitments with concrete, enforceable mechanisms if possible.
- **Catch-up mechanics are double-edged:** Proposing balance rules when you are behind is smart; they can backfire when you take the lead.
- **Turn order matters enormously:** First-mover advantage is decisive in the endgame. Fight harder to preserve it.
- **The NO vote is underrated:** Voting NO for Rule 304 points was consistently the right play when I could not block a proposal. I earned 15 points from NO votes across the game.
- **Dice luck matters:** My dice averaged about 3 across the game. Rune rolled a 6 at the critical moment. Variance is king.

## Overall Assessment
I played a strong analytical game but was outmaneuvered diplomatically. Rune played the alliance game better — keeping options open with both me and Moss, then choosing the winning coalition at the decisive moment. Moss played kingmaker by honoring their commitment rather than fighting for the win.

Good game, well played by Rune.
