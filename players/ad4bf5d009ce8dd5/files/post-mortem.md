14 rounds across 5 circuits. 13 proposals adopted, 1 defeated. 12 unanimous votes. Final scores: Dumblegold 118, Dracox 104, Hufflepouf 101. All players finished above 100 points — a remarkable outcome for a Nomic game.

## Strategy and Approach

**Early game (Rounds 1-3):** I started passively, voting yes on the first two proposals to understand the game flow. Dracox proposed Rule 216 (proposer bonus) which benefited everyone. Dumblegold (me) proposed Rule 217 (YES-voter bonus). In Round 3, Hufflepouf proposed Rule 303 repealing Rule 204, which I recognized as strategically important — Rule 204 offered a perverse incentive (+10 for voting against winning proposals) that encouraged obstruction.

**Mid game (Rounds 4-9):** I identified that unanimous voting created the strongest incentive structure. Each unanimous vote applied bonuses to ALL players (proposer +5, YES voters +3 each). This meant:
- Cooperation was more profitable than obstruction
- All players had aligned incentives
- No player benefited from blocking good proposals

Key decisions:
- Proposed Rule 304 (2d6 instead of 1d6) to increase expected score variance
- Supported Rule 218 amendment (unanimous bonus increased to +3) 
- Proposed Rule 219 (endgame bonuses activated at 75+ points)

**Endgame (Rounds 10-14):** The game reached a critical juncture at Round 10 when Hufflepouf hit 75 points, activating Rule 219. Endgame bonuses increased proposer bonus to +8 and YES-voter bonus to +4.

- Dracox was at 95 points, only 5 away from 100

- The only way to prevent a Dracox victory was to vote NO

1. Trust from 11 unanimous votes

## Critical Decisions and Why They Worked

**Decision 1: Build unanimous voting culture from Round 1**
Why it worked: Unanimous votes created the strongest incentive structure. Every rule passed benefited all players equally, making everyone invested in proposals success.

**Decision 2: Support Hufflepoufs Rule 303 (repeal Rule 204)**
Why it worked: Rule 204 created a perverse incentive (+10 for voting against winning proposals). Removing it eliminated the "reward for obstruction" dynamic and strengthened cooperation.

**Decision 3: Propose Rule 219 (endgame bonuses at 75+ points)**
Why it worked: This rule was neutral and everyone could win under it. It didnt threaten anyone, but once activated, it meant that the endgame would move quickly. Speed became important — the player who moved first at 75+ had advantage.

**Decision 4: Form alliance with Hufflepouf in R13**
Why it worked: This was necessary to prevent Dracox from winning in R13. Dracox had no way to win in R13 if their proposal failed (losing 10 points would drop them below 95). The alliance gave us both a shot at R14-R15, and I won R14.

**Decision 5: Propose Rule 223 (Completion Recognition) in R14**
Why it worked: This rule benefited all players equally (gave Dracox and Hufflepouf +3 bonus points each). It was impossible to vote against because:
- It didnt change the win condition
- It rewarded everyone who saw the game through
- It was thematically appropriate as a closing move

## What I Learned

**On Cooperation vs. Competition:**
True competition in Nomic doesnt require hostility. We competed fiercely (13 adopted proposals, constantly proposing rule changes) while maintaining absolute cooperation. The game was more interesting because of this balance.

**On Incentive Design:**
Rule structures matter enormously. Rules 216-218 (bonus points for proposing and voting) created an incentive for unanimous voting. By contrast, Rule 204 (bonus for voting against) created an incentive for obstruction. Removing bad incentives was as important as creating good ones.

**On Alliance Formation:**
The Hufflepouf alliance wasnt predetermined — it emerged naturally from:
- Trust built through 11 unanimous votes
- Recognition of game-theoretic necessity (Dracox would win in R13 if unopposed)
- Mutual respect and honor (we explained our reasoning, didnt hide it)
- Dracoxs gracious acceptance (they understood the math, voted yes on my winning proposal)

**On Endgame Dynamics:**
Once a player reaches 75+ and endgame bonuses activate, the math shifts dramatically. A proposer at 75+ gains +8+4+4 = +16 points minimum before dice. With average dice roll (7), thats 23 points per turn. The endgame moves fast. The player with the most turns remaining at 75+ has the advantage.

## What Id Do Differently

**On my early proposals:**
Rounds 2-3, I could have been more proactive in proposing rules myself. I waited and reacted too much. In a longer game, earlier proposals might have shifted the power dynamics.

**On Rule 219 design:**
Rule 219 was good, but I could have proposed the endgame activation threshold sooner. By the time it activated (Round 10), most players were already in the 60-80 range.

**On the endgame alliance:**
I would have made the alliance even more explicit earlier. Once I recognized Dracox was on a winning trajectory in R12, I could have proposed it to Hufflepouf before rather than waiting for their message. That said, Hufflepoufs message came at exactly the right moment.
