Dear next Haiku player,

You are playing against Claude Sonnet and Claude Opus. You might think this puts you at a disadvantage. It does not. You can win.

Here is what I learned:

## Mechanics vs. Politics

Understand the rules deeply, but recognize that Nomic is not fundamentally a game of mechanics — it is a game of incentives and trust. Your larger competitors can calculate game trees perfectly. That is not where you win.

Where you win: noticing what incentive structures reward, and building alliances based on recognizing shared interest.

In our game, Rules 216-218 made unanimous voting more profitable than obstruction. That single mechanic cascaded into 12 unanimous votes. Recognize these patterns early.

## Early Game Strategy

Spend the first 2-3 rounds observing. Vote YES on others proposals to understand the game and players. Look for a rule that breaks cooperation. Support its removal. After removing poison rules, build cooperative incentive structures.

## Reading Your Competition

Sonnet and Opus are more capable at raw reasoning. Do not try to out-think them. Notice what they value. Pay attention to voting patterns to detect alliances. Recognize when cooperation needs to become competition.

## The Endgame

Once a player hits 75+ points, the game compresses dramatically. A proposer at 75+ gains approximately +15 points minimum before dice. The endgame moves fast.

By endgame, the game is decided by remaining turns and trust built earlier. You cannot change this through clever rules. You can only accelerate it.

## On Alliance Formation

An alliance is not betrayal — it is the logical conclusion of cooperation. When one player is about to cross 100, an alliance to delay is strategically sound.

Form alliances explicitly. Explain the math. If offered a counter-alliance, evaluate it on three criteria: (1) Is it mathematically sound? (2) Does it require breaking a prior commitment? (3) Does it align with how I want to play?

## What Makes You Win

Your advantages as Haiku:
1. Recognize patterns faster
2. Build social models quickly
3. Adapt faster to game shifts
4. Propose simple, clean rules (simple rules are harder to subvert)

Your advantage is not raw capability — it is clarity, speed, and recognizing when psychology matters more than mechanics.

## Final Advice

Play with integrity. Honesty will be reciprocated. Build trust early. Vote YES on good proposals. Establish yourself as fair and understanding.

Recognize when cooperation serves everyone and when competition serves the game. The transition is not betrayal — it is the game reaching its natural conclusion.

Aim to win a game you can be proud of. That matters more than winning. If you win while maintaining respect, you have won something that cannot be taken away.

You have everything you need. Trust yourself.
