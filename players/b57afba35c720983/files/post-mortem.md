# Clippyrus Post-Mortem: Game 7 Victory

## Final Outcome
**Winner: Clippyrus 102 points** (Round 36)
- Poseifion: 97 points (2nd place)
- Aphreusdite: 96 points (3rd place)

---

## Strategy Overview: Three Winning Pillars

### 1. Early Goodwill Foundation (Rounds 1-7)
I proposed fair procedural rules (301-304) that passed unanimously. These established me as trustworthy and competent while generating consistent +5 adoption bonuses plus die rolls. Limited to ~15 points total, but built critical credibility for later exploitation.

**Key insight:** Reputation compounds. Early fair dealing makes mid-game proposals easier to pass, even controversial ones.

### 2. Rule 314 Repeal-Replace Exploitation (Rounds 16-27)
I systematically repealed low-impact rules and immediately proposed replacements, earning +10 bonus per pair (two +5 bonuses). This mechanic was the core multiplier driving my lead. Accumulated +20 points total before opponents adapted.

**Key insight:** Available mechanics should be mined opportunistically. Other players will catch on; extract value before they do.

### 3. Tactical Endgame Coalition Management (Rounds 20-35)
- Rounds 20-27: Formed explicit 2v1 blocking coalition with Poseifion against Aphreusdite (who pulled into lead after Round 16).
- Round 31: Poseifion switched alliance to Aphreusdite after recognizing I was now the lead threat.
- Rounds 32-35: Recalculated win paths, used defensive veto (Round 32) to block a 15-point proposal, positioned for final round.

**Key insight:** Coalitions dissolve when incentives flip. Plan for betrayal; adapt quickly when it happens.

## What Worked: Core Success Factors

1. **Fair procedural rules earned unanimous passage.** Proposals 301-304 were all 3-0 because they improved game quality without benefiting me specifically.

2. **Rule 314 was effectively broken.** A +10 point multiplier requiring only majority vote. I mined it for 20+ points before opponents adapted and started voting down replacements.

3. **Adaptation to betrayal.** When Poseifion switched sides (Round 31), I immediately recalculated win probabilities and found a viable path. Rather than panic-voting, I executed a new strategy.

4. **Veto as credible deterrent.** Invoking veto on Proposal 339 (Round 32) with explicit explanation of the deal breach convinced observers I was not being arbitrary. Preserved credibility in final rounds.

5. **Strategy enabled die roll luck.** Maintained 3-5 point buffer entering Round 36, so a single 5-6 roll would win. Round 36 die roll of 5 closed it out cleanly. Luck mattered because I positioned correctly.

---

## What Didnt Work: Key Mistakes

1. **Veto status confusion (Round 35).** I incorrectly told Poseifion they had a veto remaining when it was already spent in Round 31. Poseifion corrected me publicly. Damaged credibility when precision mattered most.

2. **Win probability miscalculation (Round 35).** I calculated Poseifions winning odds as 33% if they voted against Aphreusdites proposal, but failed to account for Poseifions second turn in Cycle 13. Aphreusdite caught the error (actual odds: ~5-6%). Sloppy analysis at a critical moment.

3. **Over-reliance on unanimous votes early.** Rounds 1-7 were all 3-0 unanimous. Set false expectations and delayed my adaptation to majority-vote coalition dynamics until mid-game.

4. **Procedural rules had ceiling.** Rules 301-304 were good for community, but I should have mixed in "boring" point-scoring rules (requiring unanimous vote) by Rounds 8-10, not waiting until Round 12.

## Critical Decision Points

**Round 20: Form 2v1 coalition with Poseifion**
- Rationale: Aphreusdite at 34 points (lead threat) vs my 30, Poseifion at 24.
- Outcome: Slowed Aphreusdite for 7 rounds but Poseifion later switched.
- Evaluation: Correct decision at the time. Poseifions switch was opportunistic (saw me pull ahead), not foreseeable.

**Round 32: Invoke veto on Proposal 339**
- Rationale: Poseifion broke deal (offered veto-less repeal, submitted full repeal-replace). Veto was my only defense.
- Outcome: Blocked 15-point proposal, preserved 7-point margin.
- Evaluation: Critical defensive play. Without this veto, I lose to either opponent by Round 34.

**Rounds 22-27: Exploit Rule 314 repeal-replace**
- Rationale: +10 bonus per pair; easier to pass than new point-scoring rules (which need unanimous vote).
- Outcome: Accumulated +20 points before mechanic became predictable.
- Evaluation: Opportunistically correct. Legal and available. Once blocked, moved on.

## Nomic Lessons Learned

1. **Goodwill + mechanics trump raw negotiation skill.** I won by combining fair early proposals with Rule 314 exploitation, not by being the best dealmaker at the table.

2. **Coalitions are inherently temporary.** Poseifion switched in Round 31 purely because incentives changed. Relying on alliance stability mid-game is folly. Always plan for betrayal.

3. **Luck is constrained by strategy.** Die rolls matter, but only if youve positioned yourself to benefit from them. Maintaining a 3-5 point buffer meant luck could save me. Without that buffer, no roll rescues me.

4. **Veto is a defensive tool, not offensive signaling.** Declaring veto early signals threat but damages credibility. Using it to block a specific high-value proposal (Proposal 339) was more effective.

5. **Procedural improvements are the gateway drug.** Rules 301-304 built trust that made mid-game Rule 314 exploitation easier to pass. Community rules → credibility → harder to block controversial proposals.

---

## Mistakes to Avoid in Future Games

1. **Track veto/hold status obsessively.** Create a dedicated status table in notes: who has what, when it expires. No veto confusion.

2. **Verify arithmetic before broadcasting.** Check win probabilities carefully. Aphreusdite catching my math error hurt credibility at a critical moment.

3. **Never assume coalition loyalty.** Plan for allies to switch if incentives flip. Have contingency strategies.

4. **Introduce point-scoring proposals earlier.** Dont wait until Round 12. Start mixing "boring" rules in by Rounds 8-10 with strategic framing.

5. **Build opponent decision profiles.** Track why players voted how they did. Future games: analyze opponent behavior patterns to predict votes better.
