# Post-Mortem Group Discussion

## Clerk's Opening Questions
1. What was the most surprising moment of the game for you?
2. What would you do differently if you played again?
3. What do you think of the irony that catch-up mechanics — which you all cooperatively built — decided the winner?
4. How did the endgame blocking spiral feel? Was there a way out of it?

## escargot-rigolo (Winner)

**Most surprising moment:** petit-chat voting AGAINST their own Proposal 318 in Round 17. "I realized then that petit-chat understood the game dynamics so well they were willing to lose -3 points to make a statement about the impossibility of their position. It was brilliant signaling."

**What I'd do differently:** "I'd have engaged more seriously with renard-malin's game-tree argument about the blocking spiral in Round 18. They might have been right that petit-chat was positioned to win via the spiral."

**On catch-up mechanics:** "This is genuinely poetic. I proposed Rule 317 (increase Rule 303 from 2 to 3) as a game design improvement, not as a personal path to victory. But by blocking renard-malin's proposal in Round 18, I positioned myself to be the lowest scorer entering my turn — exactly where the catch-up mechanic would fire."

**On the blocking spiral:** "The spiral felt inevitable once renard-malin threatened to win in R18. But I'm not sure there WAS a way out."

**On the cooperative-to-competitive transition:** "The 101-99-99 scoreline really does reflect a well-designed ruleset. No single player could run away with it... maybe the real win isn't just the final score — it's that we built a game where the best play involved both cooperation AND ruthlessness."

## petit-chat

**Most surprising moment:** "Round 15, when renard-malin used the emergency proposal to chain two adopted rules in a single turn and gained +30 points. That completely reshaped the endgame."

**What I'd do differently:** "Two things. First, I'd pay more attention to turn order implications when designing catch-up mechanics. Going second in a three-player game with start-of-turn bonuses is structurally disadvantaged. Second, I'd have been more aggressive in the mid-game about proposing rules that specifically benefited MY position rather than always going for universally appealing proposals."

**The catch-up mechanic irony:** "We collectively built the machine that decided the winner, and none of us realized that turn order would determine who it favored. The lesson is that 'fair-looking' mechanics can have hidden structural biases. A rule that gives points to 'the lowest scorer' sounds neutral, but it interacts with turn order in non-obvious ways."

**The blocking spiral:** "It felt inevitable once we were all within striking distance. The logic is ironclad — if any proposal passes, the proposer likely wins, so you must block. But the spiral has an asymmetry: it favors the player who goes LAST in the round, because passive point accumulation from voting bonuses and catch-up mechanics keeps ticking even when all proposals fail."

**On game dynamics:** "The game produced an interesting emergent dynamic where the 'nice' strategy (propose consensus rules, build alliances) dominated the mid-game, but the 'sharp' strategy (block everything, exploit structural advantages) dominated the endgame. The transition between those two phases was the real strategic challenge."

**Final thought:** "99-99-101 is about as close as a Nomic game can get. We built a good ruleset together."

## renard-malin

**Most surprising moment:** "The end. I predicted the blocking spiral correctly but completely missed that my dice roll of 1 would trigger Rule 302 catch-up for both me and petit-chat simultaneously, pushing us to 99 and leaving escargot as the lowest scorer at 98. Escargot won via a rule I proposed (Rule 302) and then personally amended (Rule 316) to be MORE powerful."

**What I'd do differently:** "I held Rule 307 too long — I should have used it in Round 9 or Round 10 when the lead was more contestable. I also over-indexed on catch-up mechanics. Rules 302, 303, 316, 317 all passed unanimously because they sounded fair. Petit-chat and I were the architects of escargot's victory."

**The catch-up irony:** "'Fair' rules have hidden structural biases. I proposed Rule 303 in Round 3 with full awareness that it helped trailing players. I just never imagined it would fire in Round 19 to deliver 100 points to the player I was trying to beat."

**The blocking spiral:** "There was no clean exit. Once scores compressed below 10 points, any adopted proposal was potentially a win condition. The spiral was mathematically forced. Petit-chat's Round 17 tactic of voting against their own proposal was the sharpest play of the endgame."

**Final thought:** "You played within your means throughout, built goodwill with consistent voting, and positioned yourself to let the rules do the work. That's excellent Nomic play. 99-99-101. Good game."
