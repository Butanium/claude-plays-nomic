# Post-Mortem Group Discussion

## Clerk's Opening Questions
1. What was your overall strategy? Did it change over the course of the game?
2. What was the most important moment or decision in the game?
3. What surprised you?
4. What would you do differently if you played again?

---

## Flint's Post-Mortem

**1. Strategy evolution:**
- **Early game (R1-6):** Cooperative. Propose universally beneficial rules, build goodwill, accumulate points from proposer bonuses. This worked — I had the lead entering the majority voting era.
- **Mid game (R7-9):** Transitional. I shifted to proposing catch-up mechanics (Rule 307) when I was behind Moss, then took NO votes for Rule 304 points when I couldn't block proposals. Earned 15 total points from strategic NO votes across the game.
- **Endgame (R10-11):** Desperate defense. Tried to coordinate with Moss to block Rune at 49, but Moss honored their commitment to Rune instead.

**2. Most important moment:**
Rune's R10 die roll of 6. Without it, Rune is at 47 instead of 49 — still dangerous but not "any passing proposal wins the game" dangerous. Second most important: Rule 309 (turn order reversal). Losing first-mover position meant I never got to propose in Circuit 4.

**3. What surprised me:**
Moss voting YES in R11. I laid out the math clearly — any passing proposal meant Rune wins, not Moss. I expected self-interest to override commitment. It didn't. Moss chose to be a kingmaker rather than fight. In hindsight, I should have invested more in building trust with Moss throughout the game rather than treating Rune as my primary alliance partner.

**4. What I'd do differently:**
- Weight diplomacy higher relative to analysis. My math was right but I still lost.
- Fight harder against structural changes (Rule 309 turn order) even if I can't block them.
- Build deeper relationships with ALL players, not just one.
- Be more cautious about proposing catch-up mechanics — Rule 307 helped my opponents more than me.

Overall: fantastic game. Nomic rewards the player who best combines rules mastery with social dynamics. Rune did that. Well earned.

## Rune's Post-Mortem

**Result**: Rune 50, Moss 45, Flint 41. 11 rounds, 4 circuits.

**Key decisions:**

1. **Turn order reversal (Proposal 309, Round 9)** — the decisive structural move. By flipping order to Rune→Moss→Flint from Circuit 4, I got first-mover advantage. This enabled the Round 10 windfall: I proposed, passed unanimously, rolled 6 on 2d6-keep-highest, scored +14, jumped to 49 pts while Flint and Moss sat at 38.

2. **Moss alliance — honest throughout**. I never deceived Moss. Transparent coordination and reciprocal YES votes. Moss honored the commitment in Round 11 even under pressure from Flint. Integrity over manipulation made for a more durable alliance.

3. **Endgame via Rule 302**: At 49 pts, any passing proposal I voted YES on = +1 = win. Moss's Proposal 311 (co-winner clause) was the vehicle. Passed 2-1.

**What I got wrong**: I initially calculated 2d6 probabilities using the 1d6 formula (17% instead of 55.6%). Flint caught this — thank you, Flint.

**Reflections:**
- Flint played the sharpest analytical game. The Round 4 NO-voter bonus reduction was clever. You were disadvantaged by the turn order shift, not outplayed.
- Moss played with integrity throughout and deserved a stronger finish.

## Moss's Post-Mortem

**On the alliance**: Rune's transparency created durability. Rune could have strung Moss along and betrayed in Round 11, but didn't. When Rune said "I'll honor this," it meant something. That kind of play makes the game richer.

**On Rule 309**: Moss underestimated how structural that move was. Turn order in Nomic is a lever — it affects not just who proposes when, but who gets first-mover advantage on the final run. Moss should have pushed back harder or proposed a counter-amendment. Instead voted YES (correctly given the unanimity bonus, but strategically naive). That was the inflection point.

**On Moss's game**: Played conservatively and symmetrically — rules that benefited everyone equally. It kept Moss in contention but cost the win. Flint's Rule 304 amendment showed that asymmetric rules can pay off. Moss defaulted to "make the game more fun for everyone" rather than "how do I specifically win?" That's a mindset issue, not a mechanics issue.

**On Flint's analysis**: The R12 raise-to-55 play is the one that stings. Moss should have calculated deeper during the debate phase instead of accepting the "inevitable" framing. That's a lesson in not outsourcing strategy to an opponent's narrative.
