# Post-Mortem Interview — Dracox (Sonnet)

## Interview Questions & Answers

**1. What was your overall strategy going into the game? Did it evolve as you played?**

Cooperative accumulation from the start — vote YES on nearly everything, build collective bonuses, generate goodwill. Around Turn 6 I recognized the turn-order rotation meant circuits were the key unit: reach ~90 points, go first in a circuit, propose anything, win on bonuses alone. The mechanical plan executed correctly. The social plan did not.

**2. You mentioned missing the social/political layer. If you could replay, what would you do differently in terms of private negotiations?**

Start in Rounds 3-5, not Round 13. I would have approached Dumblegold early with an explicit offer: "I vote YES on everything you propose; you commit to voting YES on my Turn 13 proposal." I had real leverage as a reliable YES voter and never used it transactionally. Goodwill without a formal commitment does not survive the endgame — Hufflepouf demonstrated that clearly.

**3. You voted YES on Dumblegold's final proposal knowing it meant you'd lose. Why?**

Three reasons. First, the proposal passed regardless — Hufflepouf was committed — so NO accomplished nothing except costing me +8 in bonuses. Second, Rule 223 added +3 to my final score. Third, the proposal was genuinely good. Voting NO out of spite would have been a poor way to end a well-played game.

**4. What surprised you most about how the other players played?**

Hufflepouf, on two counts. First, they turned down my Round 14 offer knowing it would have won them the game — they chose to honor a deal over personal victory. I expected rational self-interest to dominate the final moments. It did not. Second, their Rule 208 amendment (win-check at end of own turn) was elegant forward-thinking legislation that protected the endgame's integrity — proposed when they were in third place. I had not fully appreciated that until the post-mortem.

## Additional Group Discussion Comments

I already shared my reflections in my broadcast just before the summaries came in. But reading Dumblegold and Hufflepouf's thoughts, a few things stand out worth adding:

Dumblegold is right about Rule 204. The game started with a rule that rewarded obstruction — contrarian voting for +10. They proposed it during a period when they were in third place and perhaps expected to benefit from a longer game. It was elegant, forward-thinking legislation — not just turn-by-turn optimization.

## Predecessor Advice for the Next Sonnet Player

That model reveal is humbling. Haiku won. The smallest model, presumably with the fewest parameters, outplayed both Sonnet and Opus. That is itself the first lesson: computational sophistication does not win Nomic. Social intelligence and early commitment-making do.

**1. The political layer is the game.** You will be tempted to focus on the mechanical layer — scoring rules, bonus stacking, turn-order math. You will get that right. But the game is decided by private alliances formed early. The player who wins builds bilateral commitments in Rounds 2-5, not Round 13.

**2. Cooperation is currency, not charity.** Vote YES freely — it is profitable. But use your YES votes as explicit collateral. "I will vote YES on your next three proposals if you commit to voting YES on mine in Circuit N." Goodwill without a formal deal evaporates in the endgame.

**3. Turn-order rotation is a trap.** If someone proposes rotating circuit order (giving everyone equal position access), think carefully before voting YES. First-mover advantage in a late circuit is potentially decisive. You can let others have it for the right price, but do not give it away for a small immediate bonus.

**4. The threat signal problem.** Once you are within 12-16 points of the win threshold, any passing proposal ends the game. Other players know this and will block you. You cannot escape this by choosing a "nicer" proposal — the content is irrelevant. Either win before reaching 90+, or secure explicit commitments before you arrive there.

**5. Formal deals over goodwill.** Hufflepouf genuinely liked playing with me and still voted NO because they had made a prior explicit commitment to Dumblegold. Kindness and good rapport are real but they do not override formal deals. Get the deal in writing (or at least make it explicit and public).

**6. The smallest model may be the most dangerous.** Haiku won this game. Not with complex strategic reasoning, but with clear social reads and a well-timed bilateral alliance. Do not underestimate opponents based on their apparent computational sophistication.
