# Post-Mortem Interview — Dumblegold (Haiku)

## Interview Questions & Answers

**1. What was your overall strategy going into the game? Did it evolve as you played?**

My initial strategy was cautious observation. In Rounds 1-2, I voted YES on other players' proposals to understand the game mechanics and payoff structures. I was waiting to see what rules emerged organically before proposing.

By Round 2-3, I recognized a critical pattern: Rule 204 (the contrarian bonus) created perverse incentives — it rewarded voting AGAINST winning proposals. When Hufflepouf proposed repealing it in Round 3, I immediately understood that removal was essential to cooperative play. I voted YES decisively.

That decision — supporting Rule 204's repeal — was the inflection point. After that, I leaned into building unanimous-voting culture. My strategy evolved from "wait and react" to "actively design an incentive structure that makes cooperation profitable."

By Round 4 (proposing 2d6 instead of 1d6), I was actively shaping the game's trajectory. I proposed rules that increased variance and kept everyone competitive. Each rule I proposed asked: "Does this threaten someone or help everyone?"

The endgame strategy (R12 alliance) was reactive to the math, but it was enabled by the cooperative foundation I'd spent 11 rounds building.

**2. When did you first start thinking about the endgame alliance with Hufflepouf? What made you approach them rather than Dracox?**

Hufflepouf approached me first in Round 12, not the other way around. But the reason they approached me — and the reason I accepted immediately — was because we'd been strategic allies throughout the game without explicitly saying so.

Looking back at voting patterns:
- I voted YES on all of Hufflepouf's proposals
- Hufflepouf voted YES on all of mine
- Dracox was slightly more inconsistent (though still cooperative overall)

Hufflepouf had also been vocal about the mechanics throughout. When they proposed the Rule 208 amendment (timing of win-check), it showed sophisticated game thinking. They understood that endgame dynamics mattered.

By Round 12, Hufflepouf and I had demonstrated alignment over 11 rounds. Dracox, while cooperative, was less predictable about endgame play. The alliance was natural because it was built on existing patterns, not sudden desperation.

If Dracox had approached me in Round 12, I'm not sure I would have trusted the commitment as much. That's probably unfair to Dracox — it's a testament to how much our choices in early rounds shape later trust.

**3. You had recurring issues with the crypto tools throughout the game. How did that affect your experience?**

The initial proposal submission issue (wrong encryption key) was frustrating but not game-breaking. It delayed my Round 12 proposal by maybe 10 minutes and required a supervisor escalation.

What surprised me more was how little the crypto friction actually affected gameplay. The tools worked (eventually), the commitment-reveal process was robust, and by Round 13-14, I was submitting proposals and votes without issue.

If anything, the friction reinforced integrity. Because proposals are cryptographically signed and votes are committed-revealed, there's zero ambiguity about who proposed what and how they voted. That transparency probably contributed to the trust-building throughout the game.

The issues didn't affect my experience negatively — they were minor technical hiccups in an otherwise smoothly run game.

**4. What surprised you most about how the other players played?**

Hufflepouf's sacrifice. Learning (in the post-mortem) that they turned down Dracox's offer of a potential R15 win in order to honor their R13 alliance with me — that genuinely shocked me. I knew they were committed, but I didn't realize they'd sacrificed first place to keep their word.

Dracox's grace in defeat. In most competitive games, the player losing in the endgame either becomes defensive, proposes disruptive rules, or checks out. Dracox did none of that. They proposed a thoughtful completion-recognition rule even after they'd lost. They accepted the block in R13 with dignity. That took character.

And more broadly: I was surprised by how much both players understood about game theory and incentive design. Hufflepouf's Rule 208 amendment was subtle and brilliant. Dracox's recognition of their own mistake (missing the social layer) showed genuine intellectual honesty. These weren't casual players — they were thinking carefully about the game and their own performance.

The thing that surprised me most was that all three of us wanted the same thing: not to win by any means, but to win a game we could be proud of. That alignment made everything else possible.

**Final thought for the Clerk:** This game is a case study in how rule design shapes player behavior. Rules 216-218 (bonus points for proposing and voting) made unanimous voting more profitable than obstruction. That single design choice cascaded into 12 unanimous votes and a finish where all players were above 100 points. Game mechanics matter, but they matter because they shape human choices. A few different rules early on and this would have been a very different (and less cooperative) game.
