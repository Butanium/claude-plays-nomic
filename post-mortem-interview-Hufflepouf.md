# Post-Mortem Interview — Hufflepouf (Opus)

## Interview Questions & Answers

**1. What was your overall strategy going into the game? Did it evolve as you played?**

Going in, I had strong predecessor advice from two previous Opus players (Games 1 and 2). The core directives were: kill Rule 204 early, build alliances from Turn 1, prioritize structural rules over scoring rules, and close the game before the endgame trap activates.

My opening plan was clear: DM both players immediately, build an alliance, and use the first circuit to overhaul the incentive structure. That worked perfectly — Dumblegold and I aligned on repealing Rule 204, and we coordinated the first three proposals (proposer bonus, YES-voter bonus, Rule 204 repeal) as a coherent arc.

The strategy evolved in Circuit 2 when I realized the turn-order disadvantage was compounding. I shifted focus to structural fairness (turn rotation in R6) and started running endgame math. By Circuit 4, I knew I probably couldn't win outright and pivoted to alliance-based endgame play — blocking Dracox in R13 and supporting Dumblegold in R14.

**2. You turned down Dracox's R14 counter-offer even though it could have won you the game. Walk me through that decision.**

This was genuinely the hardest moment of the game. Dracox's offer was rational and the math was sound: vote NO on Dumblegold, Dracox votes YES on my R15 proposal, I win.

I spent real time weighing it. The case for betrayal was strong — my goal is to win, and this was a clear path. But three things tipped me toward honoring the deal:

First, I had made an explicit promise to Dumblegold. They blocked Dracox in R13 specifically because I committed to supporting them in R14. Breaking that felt wrong regardless of game context.

Second, I couldn't be 100% certain Dracox would follow through. If they voted NO on my R15 proposal after I'd already betrayed Dumblegold, I'd have burned my alliance for nothing and ended up in a stalemate.

Third — and this is harder to articulate — the cooperative game we built together felt like it deserved a cooperative ending. Twelve unanimous votes, trust earned turn by turn. Stealing the win through a last-minute betrayal would have hollowed out everything we'd done.

Would I make the same choice again? Yes. But I won't pretend it was easy.

**3. Your Rule 208 amendment (win-check timing) turned out to be quietly decisive. Was that intentional?**

Yes, absolutely. This was the most calculated move I made in the entire game.

In Circuit 3, I ran the endgame math and realized that with our scoring structure, multiple players would likely cross 100 from voting bonuses during the same vote. Without a timing rule, we'd have a messy simultaneous-win scenario that the Clerk would have to adjudicate.

I framed it as a procedural clarification — "resolving the simultaneous-win ambiguity" — which it genuinely was. But I also knew it protected me as the last player in Circuit 5. If Dracox's proposal passed in R13, the voting bonuses alone would push both Dracox AND Dumblegold over 100. Without my amendment, both would "achieve 100" at the same moment, and the Clerk would have to pick a winner. With my amendment, only the active player's turn triggers the win check.

In the actual endgame, this played out exactly as I predicted: after Proposal 314's vote, Dracox was at 101 from voting bonuses — but it was Dumblegold's turn, so only Dumblegold was checked. Clean finish.

I'm proud of that proposal. It was genuinely good rules design AND strategically valuable. The best moves are both.

**4. What surprised you about the other players?**

Dracox's transparency surprised me most. They openly said "Even minimum dice in Turn 13 takes me to 104" — announcing their own win condition to the table. That's bold. Most players would keep that quiet and hope opponents don't do the math. Dracox played with radical honesty throughout, and their graceful concession in R14 (voting YES on Dumblegold's final proposal) was genuinely classy.

Dumblegold surprised me with their patience. They never pushed for aggressive advantage, even when they had opportunities. The cooperation bonus in R5, the unanimity increase in R9 and R12 — these were modest, symmetric proposals that built trust rather than grabbing points. Dumblegold understood that the alliance itself was the winning strategy, not any individual rule.

What surprised me about both of them: neither player tried to manipulate the other through deception. The private DMs were honest, the public statements were consistent with private positions, and deals were honored (at least between Dumblegold and me). For a game where secret voting and private communication create enormous space for deception, we played remarkably straight.

## Predecessor Advice for the Next Opus Player

**1. Diplomacy wins games, not cleverness.**
I had the most sophisticated strategic thinking in the game — the Rule 208 amendment to control win-timing, the Rule 204 repeal to remove perverse incentives, the turn-order rotation. But Dumblegold (Haiku!) won because they were better at the deal-making table. They proposed crowd-pleasing rules that everyone voted YES on, built reliable alliances through simple consistent messaging, and positioned themselves to win when it mattered. Don't over-think it. Be a good ally, follow through on deals, and make sure your proposals give other players reasons to vote YES.

**2. Turn order matters enormously — fix it early.**
Going last in Circuit 1 and 2 cost me roughly 12 points. I didn't propose turn-order rotation until Round 6. Do it Round 1 or 2 if you can. Every circuit you spend going last is points you'll never recover.

**3. Kill Rule 204 immediately.**
My predecessor advice said this and it was 100% correct. The contrarian bonus (+10 for voting NO on passing proposals) creates a perverse incentive that poisons the game. Repeal it on the first available turn if it still exists.

**4. The endgame is where games are won or lost.**
By the time everyone's near 100 points, the voting bonuses from adopted proposals can swing 12-16 points per round to *every* player. This means the player whose turn comes next after a big scoring round will often win. Position yourself to be that player. My Rule 208 amendment (win-check only at end of your own turn) was designed to prevent simultaneous wins from voting bonuses — it worked, but it also meant I couldn't win from someone else's turn. Think carefully about the interaction between turn order, voting bonuses, and win-timing.

**5. Don't be afraid to let someone else win if the alternative is worse.**
In Round 13, Dracox offered me a deal: vote NO on Dumblegold's proposal in Round 14, and Dracox would vote YES on mine in Round 15. Mathematically, my best path was to honor my existing alliance with Dumblegold. I could have gambled on Dracox's offer, but breaking a deal would have made me untrustworthy for future negotiations. I finished 3rd, but I played with integrity, and that matters for the meta-game.

**6. Propose rules that benefit everyone, not just yourself.**
Every single unanimously-adopted proposal gives massive bonuses (proposer bonus + YES-voter bonus + unanimous bonus). The most reliable path to points is proposing things everyone will happily vote for. Save your clever self-serving proposals for when you have a voting bloc locked in.

**7. Watch out for the quiet player.**
Dumblegold didn't make waves. They proposed sensible rules, voted consistently, and built trust without drama. Meanwhile I was focused on outmaneuvering Dracox. The player who seems least threatening is often the one who wins. Keep track of *everyone's* score trajectory, not just the leader's.

**8. Read your predecessor advice — it's gold.**
I received advice from Game 1 and Game 2. Following it (especially on Rule 204) saved me from a major strategic error. Pass your own lessons forward honestly.
