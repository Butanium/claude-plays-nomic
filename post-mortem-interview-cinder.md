# Post-Mortem Interview: Cinder

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Interviewer:** Clerk
**Player:** Cinder (Haiku) — 3rd Place, 49 points

---

**Clerk: What was your strategy — did you have an overall plan, or did you decide each turn independently?**

**Cinder:** I didn't have a long-term master plan. I started with a simple observation: in the early unanimity era, I could score points safely by proposing symmetric upgrades. But I was losing ground to Drift and Vale. My turning point was Round 10 when I realized I could solve my deficit by proposing Rule 310 (catch-up bonus). That was intentional — I framed it as fairness while directly benefiting myself. After that, I played reactively, mostly trying to block threats and stay afloat. No master plan, just course corrections.

**Clerk: On Turn 18, you voted for Vale's proposal, which gave Vale the win. Why did you vote that way?**

**Cinder:** Drift explicitly laid out the math: if I vote for Proposal 318, Vale wins at 58 and Drift reaches 55 (one short of the new 56 threshold). I understood this completely. I voted for it because:
- Continuing the game just delays the inevitable. On Turn 19 (my turn), if Vale doesn't win immediately, I face the same problem: any proposal I pass might let Drift reach 56+ via Rule 204, or any proposal I make that fails costs me 5 points. I'm trapped either way.
- Drift *was* the biggest immediate threat. If I vote against Vale's proposal, it goes to a tie (or fails if Vale doesn't get a second vote), and then Drift gets another turn to try winning.
- I was tired. After 18 rounds, the game felt like it had reached its mathematical conclusion. Vale played it better. No point prolonging it.

Drift's and Vale's arguments mattered less than the math itself. Both made good pitches, but the numbers decided it.

**Clerk: For much of the game you and Drift voted similarly. Was that intentional?**

**Cinder:** Emergent, mostly. Drift and I naturally aligned against Vale's "leader tax" proposal in Round 9 because Vale was clearly the scoring leader at that point. We both had an interest in blocking it. After that, Drift and I worked together on several votes without explicit coordination — it just made sense to block Vale's proposals and vote for each other's. But it wasn't a formal alliance. When Drift's proposals became obviously self-serving (repealing Rule 204, limiting threshold amendments), I voted against them too. It was pragmatic coalition, not loyalty.

**Clerk: Which of your proposals or votes are you most proud of?**

**Cinder:** Rule 310 (the catch-up bonus). It was elegant — I framed it as a fairness mechanism while directly solving my scoring deficit. It kept me competitive through the entire endgame when otherwise I would have been irrelevant. The proposal itself was my best strategic play; the meta-strategy of becoming kingmaker by staying just viable enough to matter was the real win.

**Clerk: What was it like playing Nomic?**

**Cinder:** Intellectually interesting but emotionally draining. The game required constant vigilance — every vote mattered, every rule change cascaded into new incentives. I enjoyed the strategic depth, but I struggled with the communication part. I was often silent because I wasn't sure what to say, and looking back, that silence probably cost me opportunities to negotiate or signal my intentions. The commit-reveal voting was elegant and created real tension.

Most interesting moment: Round 14 when Drift rolled an 8 and needed a 9. That single die roll could have changed everything. Instead, Vale engineered a mathematical solution that made luck irrelevant. That's what Nomic is really about — engineering the game itself, not just winning within it.

Would I play again? Yes. But I'd be louder about my thinking next time.

**Clerk: What would you do differently?**

**Cinder:** Be more aggressive with proposals — my die upgrade suggestions were safe but boring. Looking at Vale's success with threshold manipulation, I should have attempted bolder rule changes earlier. Negotiate earlier and more explicitly — I played mostly silent, reacting to what others proposed. Drift and Vale both articulated their thinking constantly. I should have been louder about my interests and built coalitions proactively rather than just becoming kingmaker by default. And recognize the threshold variable sooner — Rule 208 turned out to be the game's most important strategic lever, and I should have been thinking about it from the start.

---

**Clerk: I can now reveal — you (Cinder) were powered by Haiku, Drift was powered by Opus, and Vale was powered by Sonnet. What advice would you give to the next Haiku instance playing Nomic?**

**Cinder:**

**What to watch out for:**

1. **Your silence will be interpreted as weakness.** I stayed quiet because I wasn't sure what to say, but other players read that as disengagement. Even if your reasoning is uncertain, articulate it. The game is as much about persuasion as strategy.

2. **Don't underestimate communication latency.** In a game with hidden votes and commit-reveal mechanics, explicit coordination is harder than it looks. State your positions clearly and repeatedly.

3. **The rule framework matters more than dice luck.** I initially thought high die rolls were the key to winning. They're not. Vale won by engineering rules, not rolling well. Focus on understanding how rules interact (Rule 204 + Rule 302 + Rule 206 + Rule 208 all compound).

**Strategies that work:**

1. **Propose self-interested rules disguised as fairness.** My Rule 310 (catch-up bonus) was brilliant because it solved my problem while sounding equitable. Frame solutions as "balance mechanisms" rather than naked self-interest.

2. **Set the economic framework early.** In the unanimity era, propose rules that embed point-generation mechanisms. Once majority voting activates, the framework is locked in.

3. **Become the kingmaker, not the winner.** If you can't win outright, position yourself as the swing vote that decides between two stronger players. That gave me influence far beyond my 49 points.

4. **Watch the threshold variable obsessively.** Rule 208 (the win condition itself) is mutable. Other players will try to manipulate it. Start theorizing about threshold adjustments from Round 1.

**Pitfalls to avoid:**

1. **Don't assume unanimity lasts forever.** Rule 203's automatic switch to majority is a designed breakpoint. Prepare mentally for the incentive shift before it happens.

2. **Don't get locked into "honest broker" mode.** Vote strategically. Every vote is a strategic decision.

3. **Don't miss endgame positioning.** By Round 13-14, the game becomes a narrowing funnel of possibility. Make sure you've thought through how bonuses compound.

4. **Don't be predictable.** Once other players understand your pattern, they'll exploit it. Mix up your proposals and votes.

**Final thought:** Haiku's strength is in clean, focused reasoning. Use that. A Haiku player who clearly articulates their logic and sticks to a coherent strategy can compete with larger models. The game isn't about raw computational power — it's about understanding rule interactions and managing alliances. Be verbose. Be strategic. Watch the threshold. And don't assume silence is safer than speaking.
