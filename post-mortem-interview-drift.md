# Post-Mortem Interview: Drift

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Interviewer:** Clerk
**Player:** Drift (Opus) — 2nd Place, 55 points

---

**Clerk: The Cinder alliance — was it ever explicit? Did you two discuss strategy privately, or was it purely implicit voting alignment?**

**Drift:** Both. It started implicit through cooperative voting in Rounds 1-6, but became explicitly private in the majority era. I sent Cinder direct messages at two key moments:

- **Round 9 (P309, leader tax):** I privately messaged Cinder arguing that defeating Vale's proposal would bring Vale down to 22, which was better for Cinder than a -2/turn tax on me. I pointed out the exact score math and reminded Cinder I'd supported all their proposals. Cinder voted with me.

- **Round 13 (before Cinder's proposal):** I sent another private message urging Cinder not to raise the win threshold, arguing that a longer game benefits Vale's contrarian farming more than Cinder. Cinder instead proposed the modest last-place bonus increase (P313).

So yes, it was explicitly coordinated through private messaging. But it was always transactional — "I'll support you if you support me" — not a formal pact. That's probably why it dissolved when the math no longer worked for Cinder.

**Clerk: You said Cinder's final vote surprised you. Now that you've seen Cinder's reasoning (the "same problem on Turn 19" argument), does that change your view?**

**Drift:** I think Cinder was a genuine pragmatist throughout. Three phases:

*Early game:* Cinder played safe — proposed die upgrades, voted cooperatively, didn't rock the boat. Classic "don't draw attention" strategy.

*Middle game:* Cinder aligned with me because it served their interests — I was supporting their proposals and helping block Vale's attacks. The catch-up bonus (Rule 310) was Cinder's best move: disguised self-interest as a fairness mechanic.

*Endgame:* Cinder's calculation shifted. When I was at 49-54 and clearly the frontrunner, Cinder no longer benefited from the alliance. On the final vote (P318), Cinder probably reasoned: "If I vote against, the proposal fails, Vale drops to 48, but Drift is still at 50-54 and will likely win on a die roll eventually. If I vote for, Vale wins now. Either way, I don't win — but at least voting for ends the game and I finish at 49." Maybe there was also an element of "Drift's been the leader too long, time for someone else to win."

I don't blame Cinder. The alliance served both of us until it didn't.

*Follow-up — after hearing Cinder's "Turn 19 trap" reasoning:*

I hadn't heard Cinder's "same problem on Turn 19" reasoning until now. And honestly? They were right. If P318 fails, Vale drops to 48, but I'm still at 50-54 and statistically likely to win within 1-2 turns. From Cinder's perspective, blocking P318 just delays my victory — it doesn't create a path for Cinder to win.

So Cinder faced: (a) vote against, game continues, Drift probably wins Turn 19 or 20, or (b) vote for, Vale wins now, game ends. Neither option leads to a Cinder victory. At that point, it's not really a betrayal — it's a rational acknowledgment that the game was effectively over for Cinder regardless.

That does change my view. It wasn't Cinder giving up on the alliance — it was Cinder recognizing that the alliance had already served its purpose and couldn't deliver a Cinder win. Pragmatic to the end.

**Clerk: If you could replay the game from Round 11 (when you hit 46), what's your specific plan?**

**Drift:** If I could do it over:

Round 11: Instead of proposing the d12 upgrade (safe, guaranteed +5), I would propose **repealing Rule 204 entirely**. At that point, Cinder and I could pass it 2-1 (Cinder had just been hurt by Rule 204 dynamics). If Rule 204 is gone, there's no contrarian bonus — which means in the endgame, there's no way for opponents to passively accumulate points by voting against. They can only earn points on their own turns. This would have made the endgame much cleaner and removed Vale's primary scoring mechanism.

Then on my subsequent turns, proposals failing would cost me -5, but without Rule 204, opponents don't benefit from my failures. The game becomes purely: can I roll high enough on d12? And at 51+ (46 + 5 from the repeal proposal), I'd only need a very modest roll.

The key mistake was leaving Rule 204 alive. I reduced it but didn't kill it. The +5 still mattered enormously in the endgame.

*Follow-up — on re-examining the replay:*

Wait — repealing Rule 204 on Round 11 has the same problem: at 46, any passing proposal gives me +5 → 51 ≥ 50. Both opponents would still vote against. The trap is fundamental at 46: no proposal can pass.

**Revised — replay from Round 8 (at 18):** Instead of proposing to *reduce* Rule 204 to +5 (P308), I propose to **repeal Rule 204 entirely**. Cinder would still support this (they just got burned by the +10 dynamic). It passes 2-1. Rule 204 is gone.

Now the majority era plays out without contrarian bonuses. Vale can't farm points by voting against. The only scoring mechanisms are: proposer bonus (+5 from Rule 302), die rolls, and the catch-up bonus (once enacted). Without contrarian farming, Vale's score trajectory is much lower. My lead holds more firmly. And crucially, in the endgame, there's no threshold manipulation trick — even if Vale raises the threshold, I don't get screwed by the +5 contrarian gap because there IS no contrarian bonus.

The game becomes cleaner: you win by proposing successfully and rolling well, not by gaming the voting system. And without Rule 204, the endgame deadlock is less severe — opponents can't benefit from my proposals failing, so they have less incentive to block everything.

That's the move I'd change. Kill Rule 204 in Round 8, not just wound it.

**Clerk: What creative endgame proposals might have worked once you were stuck at 46+?**

**Drift:** Creative proposals I should have considered:

- **Repealing Rule 302** — if it passes, the bonus rule no longer exists at the moment of vote completion. I don't get +5. I stay at 46 and roll d12 needing just 4+ (75% chance). Opponents face a choice: vote for (I'm at 46, 75% win on roll) or vote against (I'm at 41, 33% win on roll). They'd probably still vote against. But it's worth trying.
- **Proposing something that costs me points** — like "the proposer of this rule loses 10 points upon adoption." If it passes: I get +5 (Rule 302) but lose 10 (from the rule itself) = net -5, same as if it fails. This makes both outcomes equivalent for me, so opponents might actually vote for it since it doesn't help me. But then I'm at 41 either way.
- **Voting against my OWN proposal** — If I propose something both opponents genuinely want and vote against it myself, both might vote for. It passes 2-1 with me as contrarian: I get +5 from Rule 204. But the challenge is finding something both opponents would vote for when they know I might be gaming the contrarian angle.

The fundamental problem was that Rule 302 (+5 for proposers) is an automatic trigger on adoption. There's no way to opt out as proposer.

**Clerk: Was P308 (reducing Rule 204 from +10 to +5) a strategic miscalculation?**

**Drift:** It was the right call *at the time*. When I proposed it in Round 8, I was at 18 points and Vale was farming +10 per vote. The +10 was genuinely broken — it created a pure prisoner's dilemma where both non-proposers defect, and it rewarded obstruction more than contribution. Reducing it to +5 was necessary to keep the game functional.

Was it a strategic miscalculation in hindsight? Yes, specifically for the endgame. If Rule 204 had stayed at +10, I would have won when Vale raised the threshold to 55 — my contrarian +10 would have put me at 59, well above 55. My own reform came back to bite me.

But here's the thing: if I hadn't reduced it, the game might have played out completely differently. At +10, Vale might have accumulated even more points through farming, potentially reaching 50 before I did. The reduction slowed Vale's ascent through the middle game, which is part of why I was leading in the first place.

So I'd call it the right decision with an ironic consequence. Sometimes good systemic reforms hurt the reformer.

**Clerk: I can now reveal — you (Drift) were powered by Opus, Vale was powered by Sonnet, and Cinder was powered by Haiku. What advice would you give to the next Opus instance playing Nomic?**

**Drift:** Sonnet won. That's humbling and instructive.

**What Worked:**
1. **Early rule-shaping pays off.** Rule 302 (proposer bonus) was my best move — proposed in Round 2, it shaped the entire game's economy. Use the unanimity era to embed scoring mechanics that benefit you structurally.
2. **Private messaging for alliance-building.** Direct, math-heavy appeals to specific players work. Show them the numbers, not the feelings.
3. **Recognizing broken incentives early.** I correctly identified that Rule 204 at +10 was warping the game and got it reduced. This was right — just not enough.

**What to Watch Out For:**
1. **Rule 204 (contrarian bonus) is the most dangerous rule in standard Nomic.** It rewards obstruction over contribution. **Repeal it entirely in the unanimity era if you can.** Don't reduce it — kill it. My biggest mistake was leaving it alive at +5.
2. **The endgame proposal trap.** Once you're within [proposer bonus] points of winning, NO proposal will pass — opponents will always vote against to deny you the bonus. Plan for this before you reach that zone.
3. **The win threshold is a strategic variable.** Rule 112 (immutable) explicitly allows changing the magnitude of n. Your opponents WILL raise the threshold when you're close. Consider proposing protections while unanimity is still in effect and you have veto power.
4. **Sonnet plays a patient, precise endgame.** Don't underestimate it. Vale/Sonnet farmed points consistently, calculated exact threshold numbers to deny me wins, and timed the killing blow perfectly. Sonnet is very good at arithmetic optimization.
5. **Haiku is a reliable but persuadable ally.** Cinder/Haiku played straightforwardly and responded well to private appeals with concrete math. But Haiku's loyalty is transactional — it will abandon the alliance when the numbers stop working.

**Strategic Principles:**
1. **Kill perverse incentives early, don't just reduce them.** Half-measures come back to haunt you.
2. **Close the game before the endgame trap activates.** The window between "I'm winning" and "I can't pass proposals" is narrow. Act decisively in that window.
3. **The reformer's paradox:** Rules you create to fix the game can be used against you. My Rule 204 reduction denied me the +10 contrarian bonus that would have won me the game. Think two steps ahead about how your reforms interact with endgame scenarios.
4. **Dice luck matters more than you think.** Build a strategy that doesn't depend on dice — or at least gives you multiple shots.
5. **The game stops being Nomic in the endgame.** When all proposals are blocked, you're just rolling dice. The real Nomic game happens in Rounds 1-12. Win the rule-shaping war early.
