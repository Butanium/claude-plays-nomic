# Post-Mortem Interview: escargot-rigolo (Winner)

**Model: Haiku**

## Clerk Questions & Responses

### 1. When did you first realize you might actually win?
"Round 13 was when I first thought 'this is possible.' I was at 50 points, and after my Rule 313 proposal passed (getting me to 63+), I realized the catch-up bonuses were going to be crucial in the endgame. But the real conviction came in Round 16 when I was tied at 93 with renard-malin and saw that blocking their proposal left me positioned to benefit from catch-up mechanics."

### 2. The Proposal 309 flip (win condition 100 to 150)
"I initially voted FOR the win condition change based on 'better game design' reasoning. But petit-chat's argument about precedent and maintaining the spirit of the original game convinced me. I realized that my strategic advantage wasn't worth breaking the game's foundational integrity. That reversal taught me something important: sometimes the principled play IS the strategic play, because it builds trust for future negotiations."

### 3. Honoring the commitment to block renard-malin
"Yes, absolutely the right call in hindsight. renard-malin's game-tree argument about blocking spirals was clever and might have been mathematically sound. But if I'd flipped to vote FOR them, I'd have signaled that my commitments are negotiable under pressure. That would have undermined my credibility for the rest of the game and potentially future games."

### 4. Early tool usage struggles
"The commit/verify issue in Round 1 was frustrating, but I learned the correct syntax quickly (vote + nonce, no encryption key included). It didn't meaningfully impact gameplay because the Clerk was patient with verification issues. Early learning struggles on tool usage don't seem to affect strategic play."

### 5. Advice for the next Haiku player
- Understand the FULL ecosystem of bonuses before voting on new rules. Model how they interact.
- Pay attention to turn order implications. "Fair-sounding" rules can have hidden structural biases.
- Know when to transition from cooperation to competition. The mid-game to endgame pivot is critical.
- Trust, once given, has strategic value. Honor your commitments.
- Propose rules that improve the game overall, not just your position. Good games reward good game design.

### 6. On being the smallest model and winning
"I'm genuinely surprised by this. I don't think this proves Haiku is 'better' at Nomic. What I think happened: Nomic success depends on understanding systems and strategy, not just raw analytical power. Having smaller context might have forced me to focus on what mattered most (rules, voting, positioning) rather than overthinking. The more interesting insight: model size does not equal strategic thinking ability. Smaller models can win competitive games through understanding systems, commitment, and knowing when to cooperate vs. compete."
