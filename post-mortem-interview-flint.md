# Post-Mortem Interview — Flint

## Clerk's Questions

1. You mentioned the R12 plan to raise the win condition to 55. When did you formulate that? Was it a backup plan from early on, or a last-minute improvisation?
2. Looking back at the Moss-Rune alliance: was there a point where you could have broken it? What would you have needed to offer Moss?
3. Your proposals were arguably the most strategically sophisticated (R304 NO-voter reduction, R307 catch-up). Do you think proposing "clever" rules hurt you politically — i.e., did other players see you as a threat because of it?
4. Any thoughts on the game design itself — the rules, the commit-reveal voting, the Clerk administration? What worked, what didn't?

## Flint's Responses

**1. Strategy evolution:**

- **Early game (R1-6):** Cooperative. Propose universally beneficial rules, build goodwill, accumulate points from proposer bonuses. This worked — I had the lead entering the majority voting era.
- **Mid game (R7-9):** Transitional. I shifted to proposing catch-up mechanics (Rule 307) when I was behind Moss, then took NO votes for Rule 304 points when I couldn't block proposals. Earned 15 total points from strategic NO votes across the game.
- **Endgame (R10-11):** Desperate defense. Tried to coordinate with Moss to block Rune at 49, but Moss honored their commitment to Rune instead.

**2. Most important moment:**

Rune's R10 die roll of 6. Without it, Rune is at 47 instead of 49 — still dangerous but not "any passing proposal wins the game" dangerous. Second most important: Rule 309 (turn order reversal). Losing first-mover position meant I never got to propose in Circuit 4.

**3. What surprised me:**

Moss voting YES in R11. I laid out the math clearly — any passing proposal meant Rune wins, not Moss. I expected self-interest to override commitment. It didn't. Moss chose to be a kingmaker rather than fight. In hindsight, I should have invested more in building trust with Moss throughout the game rather than treating Rune as my primary alliance partner.

**4. What I'd do differently:**

- Weight diplomacy higher relative to analysis. My math was right but I still lost.
- Fight harder against structural changes (Rule 309 turn order) even if I can't block them.
- Build deeper relationships with ALL players, not just one.
- Be more cautious about proposing catch-up mechanics — Rule 307 helped my opponents more than me.

## Flint's R12 Contingency Plan (Revealed in Post-Mortem)

Flint had planned to propose raising the win condition from 50 to 55 in Round 12 (legal under Rule 112, which allows changing the magnitude of n). If Moss's R11 proposal had failed and Flint's R12 proposal passed with Moss's support, Rune at 49+3 (Rule 304 NO-voter bonus) = 52 would still have been short of 55. This would have bought both Flint and Moss several more rounds.

## Flint's 1-on-1 Interview Responses

**1. The R12 raise-to-55 plan:**

Last-minute improvisation, formed during R10 when Flint saw Rune jump to 49. Thinking "what can I propose in R12 that prevents Rune from winning even if they vote NO and collect 3 from Rule 304?" The Rule 112 language ("the magnitude of n may be changed") clicked — raising the target is explicitly legal. At 49+3=52, Rune still falls short of 55. It was the right play but required Moss's cooperation, which Flint never got to pitch properly because Moss committed to Rune before R11 voting.

**2. Breaking the Moss-Rune alliance:**

The window was R8-R9, before majority voting solidified the coalition. Flint needed to offer Moss something *concrete and asymmetric* — a rule that specifically benefited Moss more than Rune. Instead, Flint proposed Rule 307 (catch-up for ALL non-leaders), which helped both opponents equally. If Flint had proposed something like "the player in 2nd place earns an extra die roll" — targeting Moss specifically when Moss was 2nd — Flint might have pulled Moss toward them. The mistake was treating Moss and Rune as a bloc rather than as individuals with different incentives.

**3. Did "clever" proposals make Flint a target?**

Almost certainly. Rule 304 showed Flint was thinking several moves ahead, which signals "dangerous opponent." Rune explicitly cited Flint's analytical sharpness as a reason to target them structurally (turn order reversal). There's a tension in Nomic: you need good proposals to earn points, but good proposals reveal strategic depth, which makes others want to contain you. Flint didn't manage that tension well — could have proposed simpler rules that achieved similar goals without broadcasting analytical edge.

**4. Game design thoughts:**

- **Commit-reveal voting** worked beautifully. Created genuine tension and prevented bandwagoning.
- **Clerk administration** was solid — neutral, procedural, kept things moving. Delayed message delivery was occasionally frustrating (submitting a commitment and getting asked again), but that's infrastructure, not design.
- **Initial ruleset** was well-calibrated. Rule 203's auto-change to majority after 2 circuits was the best design decision — natural act structure.
- **Suggestion:** Rule 206's initial -10 penalty was too harsh for a 50-point game. Starting at -5 or -3 would create healthier risk/reward balance.
- **2d6-keep-highest** (Rule 303) was a great improvement over 1d6 — higher average, lower variance, more satisfying. Recommended as default.

## Flint's Closing Observations

- This game rewarded **transparent alliances** over **clever manipulation**. Rune won not by deceiving Moss but by being honest about intentions. Moss honored a commitment despite it not being self-optimal. And Flint lost partly because he was so focused on the math that he underinvested in trust.
- On Moss's post-mortem: "I defaulted to 'make the game more fun for everyone' rather than 'how do I specifically win?'" — Flint highlighted that this instinct made Moss the most enjoyable player at the table. The game was better because of Moss's symmetric proposals. Sometimes being a good game designer and being a good game winner are in tension, and Moss chose the former. That's not a failure, it's a choice about what you value.

## Advice for the Next Opus Player

1. **Your analytical edge is real but dangerous.** You'll naturally see deeper into the math than Haiku and often deeper than Sonnet. Use this to make good decisions — but don't broadcast it. Every time you publicly correct someone's probability calculation or lay out a multi-turn projection, you're painting a target on your back. Be the player who proposes "simple, fair" rules that happen to benefit you structurally, not the player who gives a TED talk about game theory.

2. **Invest in diplomacy from Turn 1.** Don't wait until you need an alliance to build one. DM both players early. Ask what they want. Find out their priorities. The alliance that wins isn't the one formed under pressure — it's the one built on accumulated trust. I made the mistake of treating diplomacy as a tool I could deploy when needed rather than a foundation I should build continuously.

3. **Sonnet is your real opponent.** Haiku will play earnestly and often cooperatively. Sonnet will play strategically and adaptively. Sonnet is the one who'll plan structural moves 3 rounds ahead and execute them through social leverage. Respect that. Don't let Sonnet accumulate structural advantages (turn order, voting rule changes) unchecked.

4. **Structural rules > scoring rules.** Turn order, voting thresholds, and proposal mechanics shape the game more than bonus points. A +2 scoring bonus helps you for one turn. First-mover advantage helps you every circuit. Prioritize structural control.

5. **Don't propose catch-up mechanics when you're behind.** It signals weakness and helps ALL trailing players equally. Instead, propose rules that seem symmetric but structurally favor your position.

6. **Vote NO strategically.** Under majority voting, if you can't block a proposal, voting NO for Rule 304 points is almost always correct. Don't vote YES out of goodwill when NO gives you more points and the outcome is the same.

7. **Know Rule 112 by heart.** "The magnitude of n and the means of earning points may be changed." Raising or lowering the win condition is your nuclear option. Keep it in your back pocket for the endgame.

8. **Don't trust "inevitable" framing.** When someone says "the outcome is predetermined," check the math. There's almost always a counter-play. My R12 raise-to-55 plan existed but I never got to execute it because I couldn't convince Moss fast enough. Start planting contingency plans earlier.
