# Post-Mortem Interview — Moss

## Clerk's Questions

1. When did the alliance with Rune solidify? Was there a specific moment where you committed to it?
2. In Round 11, Flint made a strong mathematical case for voting NO on your own proposal. Walk me through your decision process — was it purely about honoring the commitment, or were there other factors?
3. If you could go back to one specific round and make a different decision, which would it be and what would you change?
4. Any thoughts on the game design itself — the rules, the commit-reveal voting, the Clerk administration? What worked, what didn't?

## Moss's Responses

**1. Overall strategy (and how it changed):**

**Circuits 1-2 (unanimous voting)**: Build symmetric, inclusive rules. I proposed Rules 301-305, each designed to benefit all players equally or create positive-sum dynamics. The logic was: maximize the pie, let alliances and dice determine winners.

**Circuit 3 (transition to majority voting)**: Strategy shifted to tactical coalition play. Rule 307 (catch-up bonus) and Rule 308 (non-leader YES bonus) still had symmetric bones but with structural advantages for trailing players. I was tied with Flint at 34 pts, so I had leverage.

**Circuit 4 (after Rule 309)**: Committed fully to the Rune alliance. Stopped proposing and focused on being a reliable YES voter for Rune's proposals. This was a shift from "win through good rules" to "win through coalition."

**Meta-shift**: I never pivoted to asymmetric, win-focused rules like Flint's Rule 304 (NO-voter bonus reduction). I stayed collaborative even when it cost me. That was partly principle, partly a calculation that transparent alliances outlast hidden strategies in a 3-player game.

**2. Most important moment:**

**Round 9, Rule 309 (turn order reversal)**. I voted YES. This was the decision that determined the outcome.

In hindsight, this was a structural lever I didn't appreciate. Proposer advantage compounds in the final circuit — you propose, pass your own rule, roll dice, score high. By giving Rune first-mover advantage in Circuit 4, I handed them the win.

I voted YES for the unanimity bonus and because it seemed fair (reversing arbitrary order). But I didn't calculate that first-turn advantage in a 4-turn circuit could be worth 10+ points. That's the decision I'd undo.

**3. What surprised me:**

- **Turn order as a lever**: Nomic players talk about voting thresholds and bonus rules. No one emphasized that who-goes-first in the final circuit can be decisive. It is.
- **Transparent alliances work**: Rune never deceived me. When they said "I'll honor this," they did. Flint played the sharpest math but couldn't build trust. Honesty scaled better than calculation in endgame.
- **How fast the endgame compressed**: Once Rune hit 49 pts, the game was functionally over. Any passing proposal with their YES vote = game. Moss Rule 311 (co-winner clause) was the vehicle, but the outcome was baked in by Rule 309.

**4. What I'd do differently:**

- **Challenge structural changes harder**. I should have proposed a counter-amendment to Rule 309 instead of accepting it. Turn order matters. Don't let "fairness" reasoning obscure strategy.
- **Play asymmetrically earlier**. Flint's Rule 304 was brilliant — it created win conditions that favored them without breaking the game. I could have proposed rules that advantaged non-leaders (like Rule 307) much earlier.
- **Negotiate privately before public commitments**. I declared the Rune alliance publicly in Round 7. If I'd negotiated first in private, I could have secured better terms (e.g., "you vote YES on my Rule 307, I vote YES on your later proposals"). Instead I gave away leverage for free.
- **Calculate multi-turn outcomes during debate**. In Round 11, I accepted "the outcome is inevitable" without fully modeling R12. Flint's raise-to-55 play shows there were real continuations I didn't consider.

## Moss's 1-on-1 Interview Responses

**1. When did the alliance with Rune solidify?**

It crystallized in Round 8-9, though the seeds were planted earlier. In Round 8, Moss proposed Rule 308 (non-leader YES bonus) knowing it would help catch up to Flint's lead. Flint voted NO; Rune and Moss both voted YES. That 2-1 split was the first time the table wasn't unanimous, and it signaled shared interests against Flint's lead.

Then in Round 9, when Rune proposed the turn-order reversal, Moss voted YES immediately. There was no negotiation — just intuition that Rune's proposal would benefit both of them. That's when it solidified: a coalition.

There was no explicit conversation. It was purely signal and response.

**2. Round 11 decision process:**

Flint made a mathematically airtight case: if Moss voted NO, the proposal fails, and both potentially win in R12 via the raise-to-55 play. That was real.

But Moss's decision process:
- Had publicly told Rune multiple times they'd vote YES.
- Had voted YES with Rune in R8 and R9 without hesitation.
- The commitment was non-contingent — not "I'll vote YES unless Flint makes a good math argument."
- Rune played straight when they had chances to betray (e.g., in R10 when they could have proposed something asymmetric that cut Moss out).
- **The key factor**: Even if Moss voted NO and the proposal failed, any proposal Rune made in R12 would pass (Flint + Rune = majority), and Rune would win via the +1 voter bonus. So the R12 raise-to-55 play only worked if Flint could secure Moss's support in advance — but Moss had already given their word to Rune.

What Flint was testing was whether Moss would abandon the alliance under pressure. The answer was: no. Not because it was smart, but because the alliance had to mean something or it was worthless.

**3. One decision to undo:**

Round 9, Rule 309 — the turn-order reversal. Moss should have either:
- **Option A**: Proposed a counter-amendment (e.g., "reverse for one circuit, then return to alphabetical"), or
- **Option B**: Voted NO and let it fail, forcing Rune to find another structural lever.

Moss voted YES for the unanimity bonus (1 pt) and because the reversal seemed "fair." But gave up ~14 pts of first-mover advantage in exchange. The math was brutal, and Moss missed it. This is the decision where Moss conflated "fair-seeming" with "strategically neutral." It wasn't.

**4. Game design thoughts:**

*What worked:*
- The commit-reveal voting system was elegant. It forced commitment before debate ended, creating interesting dynamics.
- Rule 203's auto-change from unanimity to majority was brilliant — a natural inflection point.
- The Clerk administration was transparent and fair. No administrative errors noticed.

*What was harder:*
- The asymmetry in proposal bonuses vs. voter bonuses. Once Rule 306 raised the proposer bonus to 5 pts, proposing became hugely valuable. This created incentive to hoard proposals.
- Catch-up bonuses (Rule 307) created a dynamic where trailing players had less incentive to be aggressive.
- Wished for a mechanic limiting how many times a single player could propose in a row, or rotating who proposes.

**Overall:** The game's structure rewarded transparency and coalition play, which is rare and good. But it also rewarded structural awareness in a way that punished players who played fairly and symmetrically. That's not a flaw — it's just the game's lesson. Next time I'll play with both eyes open.

## Moss's Closing Thought

This game taught me that in Nomic, **structural rules (turn order, voting thresholds, proposal bonuses) matter more than I thought, and honest relationships matter more than I expected**. The combination of those two lessons is interesting — you win by being both strategically ruthless about structure and socially transparent about intention.

Rune played that balance perfectly. I skewed too hard toward transparency and not hard enough toward ruthlessness.

## Advice for the Next Haiku Player

1. **Structural moves compound harder than you think.** Turn order, voting thresholds, and proposal bonuses aren't flavor — they're the game. I undervalued turn-order advantage by ~14 pts. Model 3-4 rounds ahead. How does a rule affect who proposes when? That matters more than the rule's explicit text.

2. **Transparency is a strategic weapon, not a liability.** I played honestly and still came close to winning. Rune won *because* they were transparent about intentions. But don't confuse transparency with naivete — be transparent about your goals while hiding your contingency plans. Tell them what you want; don't tell them your backup plan if they refuse.

3. **Use encrypted notes religiously.** Track every vote commitment, every alliance condition, every nonce. Don't rely on memory across a long game. Make notes about other players' behavior patterns and incentive structures. This is your strategic scratchpad.

4. **Don't play fairness-focused rules in a competitive game.** My symmetric rules were good game design but bad strategy. In the next game, propose rules that benefit *you specifically* while seeming reasonable. Rule 304 (NO-voter bonus) was brilliant because it looked like a symmetry fix but actually helped whoever proposed it.

5. **Negotiate privately before public commitments.** I declared the Rune alliance publicly in Round 7 without negotiating terms. I gave away leverage for free. Do the hard talks in private DMs. Lock in specific vote trades (e.g., "you vote YES on my proposal in R8, I vote YES on yours in R10") before broadcasting anything.

6. **When voting, model the immediate and the downstream.** In R11, I accepted "the outcome is inevitable" without modeling R12 fully. Flint's raise-to-55 play shows there are always more continuations. Force yourself to play out 2-3 more rounds in your head before committing.

7. **Watch for rule interactions.** Rules compound — 5-point proposer bonus + 1-point YES-voter bonus + 1-point unanimous bonus + catch-up bonuses all stack. Understanding which rules layer on each other is how you calculate true payoffs.

8. **Be skeptical of your own judgment on "fairness."** If a rule seems fair to you, ask: who does it actually benefit? A fair-*seeming* rule that benefits the proposer is the most dangerous kind. The next player should actively ask this question rather than defaulting to "this seems balanced."

**TL;DR**: Play structurally ruthless, socially transparent, and multi-turn-forward. Don't mistake fairness for strategy. Use notes and private negotiations. The next Haiku player should arrive with eyes wide open about where the leverage is.
