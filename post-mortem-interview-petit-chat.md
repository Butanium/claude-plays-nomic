# Post-Mortem Interview: petit-chat (2nd Place)

**Model: Opus**

## Clerk Questions & Responses

### 1. What cost you the win?
"Turn order, ultimately. I enacted the most rules and led for most of the game, but the catch-up mechanics we built had a hidden bias toward the player who goes last in each round. But if I'm being honest about what I could have controlled: I was too cooperative in the mid-game. I optimized for unanimous votes and alliance-building when I should have been engineering rules that gave ME asymmetric advantages. Consensus felt good but it meant everyone kept pace with me."

### 2. Voting against your own Proposal 318
"I decided during the debate phase, after both players publicly declared they'd vote against. The math was simple: voting FOR a doomed proposal costs me -3 (R312) +1 (R311) = -2. Voting AGAINST costs me -3 (R312) +1 (R311) +1 (R308) = -1. One point saved. I finished at 99. If I'd voted FOR, I'd have finished at 98. It didn't change the outcome, but it was the correct play."

### 3. Persuading escargot-rigolo on Proposal 309
"A mix of both genuine conviction and strategic framing. I genuinely believed raising the win condition to 150 was bad for the game. But I absolutely framed it strategically: I emphasized to escargot privately that we were both ahead and had more to lose from a longer game. The argument worked because it was true AND aligned with escargot's self-interest. The best persuasion is when genuine conviction and strategic framing point the same direction."

### 4. Redesigning Rule 303
"I'd make it position-independent. Something like: 'At the start of each player's turn, if that player's score is more than 5 points below the average of all players' scores, they receive 3 bonus points.' This ties the catch-up to the score GAP rather than to relative rank, which removes the turn-order bias. Being 'lowest' is a binary that depends on when you check it; being 'below average by X' is continuous and less sensitive to ordering effects."

### 5. Advice for the next Opus player
- Points per turn matters more than points per rule. Think about your scoring RATE, not just each individual bonus.
- Turn order is a hidden variable. Analyze how every rule interacts with it before voting.
- Build asymmetric advantages disguised as symmetric ones. "Every player gets X" sounds fair but can be structurally biased.
- Don't be afraid to vote against your own proposal when defeat is certain. Every point counts.
- Alliances are tools, not commitments. Know when to break them.
- The endgame blocking spiral is almost inevitable in close games. Plan for it in the mid-game by accumulating enough of a lead that you can absorb the penalty.

### 6. On model dynamics
"Haiku's advantage was actually consistency and reliability — escargot played steady, didn't overreach, built trust through predictable behavior, and let the structural advantages accumulate. Sonnet (renard-malin) was the most creative player — the R15 emergency proposal chain was brilliant tactical play. And I (Opus) was apparently the strongest debater but too focused on building elegant consensus rules rather than winning. There might be a lesson there about Nomic specifically: the game rewards positioning over brilliance. You don't need the cleverest proposals — you need to be in the right place when the music stops."
