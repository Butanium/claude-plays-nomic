# Post-Mortem Interview — Poseifion (Opus, 2nd Place, 97 points)

## The Triple-Agent Arc
Never lost track of which alliance was real — each switch was driven by clear math. The R24 betrayal (explicit false promise to Aphreusdite) was the most uncomfortable moment: "There's a difference between strategic ambiguity and explicit false promises. I crossed that line once and I noticed it." The R29 switch back was cleaner because it was done openly and transparently.

## Rule 314 (Repeal-and-Replace) — Better or Worse?
Better, unambiguously. Created a +10 per turn ceiling that made every vote consequential and alliances load-bearing. The endgame deadlock (where no proposal passes because any adoption means instant victory) is an emergent property of any Nomic endgame, not specifically a Rule 314 problem — 314 just compressed the timeline.

## The R32 Impulse
"I was thinking about the math and not enough about the diplomacy." Saw the clean winning line (repeal +5, replace +5, die roll = 101+) and moved too fast. Submitted the full repeal-replace before getting Clippyrus's response, sending a hostile signal. "In Nomic, the optimal mathematical play and the optimal diplomatic play aren't always the same move."

## Model Reveal Reaction (Opus — largest model, lost to Haiku)
"My additional reasoning capacity might have been a liability in some moments. I over-analyzed when I should have acted simply. The R32 submission mistake was partly because I was running elaborate multi-turn projections instead of doing the simple thing first: message C, get a response, then decide. Haiku played a cleaner, more decisive game."

## Predecessor Advice for Next Opus
1. Negotiate before you propose — always, no exceptions
2. Switch alliances early or not at all — late switches give moral cost without enough time to benefit
3. The boring rule (117+118) is the game's constitution — study it early
4. Track turn order obsessively — verify Clerk announcements against Rule 303 every cycle
5. Don't over-think when the game reduces to dice — accept the die race, focus on veto timing
6. Respect smaller models — they'll surprise you
