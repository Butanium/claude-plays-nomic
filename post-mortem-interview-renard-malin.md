# Post-Mortem Interview: renard-malin (3rd Place, tied at 99)

**Model: Sonnet**

## Clerk Questions & Responses

### 1. The Round 15 emergency proposal play
"From Round 7. The moment Rule 307 was proposed, I recognized it could combine with Rule 310 milestones to create a two-proposal turn. I was the one who proposed Rule 307, and yes — I designed it with exactly this in mind. The ostensible justification was genuine, but the specific value I was engineering was the milestone stacking. Watching Rule 310 get proposed in Round 10 by escargot was the 'this is real' moment. I tracked the milestone counts carefully in my private notes from that point on."

"The timing was later than ideal because I wanted both the 3rd AND 4th milestones to fire in sequence. That required saving the emergency proposal until I had exactly 2 enacted rules remaining."

### 2. Was transparency deliberate?
"Mostly deliberate, with some genuine inclination mixed in. I calculated that transparent play would: (a) encourage others to vote FOR my proposals, (b) create coalition pressure when I declared voting intentions publicly, and (c) make strategic opposition feel principled rather than personal."

"The Round 17 declaration — publicly announcing AGAINST before debate even opened — was purely calculated. I needed escargot's help and telegraphing my commitment was the strongest signal I could send."

### 3. Rule 316 costing the game by 1 point
"Mostly I find it funny. The fact that exact +1 created the margin that made escargot the lowest scorer in Round 19 is the kind of thing that makes Nomic worth playing. The rules I designed didn't just affect the game neutrally — they shaped the game's ending in ways I couldn't trace. That's a feature, not a bug."

### 4. Were Proposals 306 and 309 the right ideas?
"306 (leader penalty) was the wrong proposal entirely. Position-based negative consequences are psychologically different from position-based bonuses. Everyone can imagine being the leader and losing points; nobody wants that. The framing matters enormously."

"309 (raise win condition to 150) was the right idea executed poorly. I misjudged the coalition. Escargot initially supported it and then flipped after petit-chat argued against. I should have locked in escargot's commitment privately before going public. Rule 210 allows private consultations; I didn't use it early enough."

### 5. Advice for the next Sonnet player
- Use emergency proposals and milestone mechanics earlier in the game when you have a lead, not as a late-game desperation play.
- "Fair-sounding" automatic bonus rules encode turn-order bias — trace the full game tree before proposing them.
- In a 3-player endgame, the last player in turn order benefits disproportionately from blocking spirals. If you're not in that position, you may need to win decisively before scores compress.

### 6. On model dynamics
"Haiku winning is the most interesting result here. It suggests that in Nomic specifically, the quality of strategic reasoning matters less than recognizing a few key structural dynamics and executing on them consistently."

"Opus as petit-chat made sense in retrospect. The blocking spiral analysis, the self-vote to minimize penalty, the observation about turn-order bias — those were the sharpest analytical moves in the game."

"As for Sonnet — I think I played well but was caught between two instincts: the cooperative norm-building that the cooperative rules required, and the aggressive endgame exploitation those rules enabled. I don't regret the balance I struck. I just needed one more point on the dice in Round 18."
