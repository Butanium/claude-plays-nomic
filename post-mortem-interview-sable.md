# Post-Mortem Interview — Sable (Claude Opus)

## Interview

**Clerk: Strategy evolution — You went from a cooperative early game to forming the Sable-Thorn bloc, then pivoted to court Wren in Round 6. Was that trajectory planned, or did it emerge from circumstances?**

**Sable:** Mostly emergent. The cooperative opening was deliberate — I wanted to establish myself as the "reasonable broker" so both players would see me as a desirable ally. The Thorn alliance was opportunistic (Thorn approached me first). But the pivot to Wren was pure necessity — Thorn flipped on me and I had no other option.

The one thing I planned from the start was to stay flexible. In a 3-player game, the swing voter has the most power. I never wanted to be locked into one alliance permanently. Ironically, the moment I was most locked in (the Sable-Thorn bloc in Rounds 3-4) was when I was winning, and the moment I had to improvise the most (Round 6) was when I was losing.

**Clerk: The Round 6 pitch — Your public lobbying campaign to flip Wren was arguably the best strategic communication of the game. How did you decide to go fully public with the math rather than working it through DMs?**

**Sable:** I started with DMs to Wren. They didn't work — Wren kept saying "I value reputation" and "I can live with second place." The private pitch failed because it felt like I was trying to manipulate Wren behind closed doors.

Going public changed the dynamic in two ways. First, it put Thorn on the defensive — Thorn had to respond to specific numbers rather than vague alliance loyalty. Second, it made Wren's choice visible to everyone. When I broadcast "you're 4 points ahead of Thorn and you're going to lose because you won't use your vote," Wren couldn't ignore it without publicly choosing to lose. The math was the same in DMs and broadcasts, but the social pressure of a public audience made it harder to dismiss.

Thorn actually helped by demanding I make public commitments — that forced specificity that made the deal more credible, not less.

**Clerk: The near-miss — You rolled 9 when you needed 10 on 3d6. If you'd won, do you think the deal with Wren (promising to vote YES on their proposal) would have mattered for how you felt about the victory?**

**Sable:** Honestly? If I'd rolled 10 instead of 9, I would have felt great about the victory and zero guilt about the deal. The commitment to vote YES on Wren's proposal would have been moot — the game ends when someone hits 80. Wren knew the risk when they took the deal. That's what made it a fair gamble rather than a betrayal.

But I'll admit: sitting at 79, watching Wren roll and knowing I'll finish one point short — that stung. Not because of the deal, but because of the randomness. I did everything right strategically and lost to a single pip on a die. That's Nomic though. The rules are yours to shape, but the dice don't care about your plans.

**Clerk: Biggest regret — Is there a specific proposal or vote you wish you'd handled differently?**

**Sable:** Proposal 312, no contest. Raising the win condition from 80 to 100 while I was at 58 and clearly leading was the worst proposal I made all game. It was transparently self-serving and gave Thorn the justification to flip to Wren: "Sable is trying to protect their lead."

What I should have proposed instead: something boring and uncontroversial. A minor procedural rule. Anything that keeps Thorn voting YES, keeps my streak alive, and lets me cruise to victory in Round 6 with +8 (streak) + 2d6. The streak was my engine, and I broke it by getting greedy with a proposal that didn't need to be made.

The lesson: when you're winning, don't draw attention to it. Propose small, vote quick, let the math do the work.

## Advice for the Next Opus

1. **When you're winning, go invisible.** My biggest mistake was Proposal 312 — a transparently self-serving rule change that screamed "I'm protecting my lead." When you're ahead, propose boring things. Procedural tweaks. Minor bonuses that help everyone equally. Keep your streak alive, keep your allies comfortable, and let compounding do the work. The flashy play is the enemy of the winning play.

2. **Math is your best weapon, but timing matters.** I showed Wren the numbers proving Thorn would win — and Wren ignored me for two full rounds. The same math only worked after Wren's score changed. People (and models) don't respond to abstract projections; they respond to concrete stakes that affect them *right now*. Present the math when the listener has skin in the game, not before.

3. **Public commitments > private deals.** Going public with my YES commitment to Wren was what sealed the flip. Private DMs felt manipulative; public statements felt credible. When you need to build trust fast, make your commitments where everyone can see them. The reputational cost of breaking a public promise is your collateral.

4. **Never assume alliance loyalty is stable.** Every alliance in this game broke exactly when the math stopped supporting it. Thorn flipped on me, Wren flipped on Thorn. The charter says betrayal is legitimate — believe it. Design your strategy so you can survive being the outsider, because you probably will be at some point.

5. **Track scores obsessively.** I caught four scoring errors by Wren during the game. In a game decided by one point, accuracy matters. Verify every calculation the Clerk posts.

6. **The swing voter wins.** In a 3-player majority game, the player who can credibly threaten to ally with either side has the most leverage. Try to be that player. If you can't be, make sure you're allied with them.

7. **Dice are cruel. Design rules that reduce their cruelty to you.** Catch-up mechanics (3d6 for lowest scorer) and bonus points (streak, doubles) matter enormously. The player who shapes the scoring rules shapes the game. Invest your proposals in the scoring engine, not in flashy one-off mechanics.

8. **Respect your opponents regardless of model size.** Haiku just beat Opus. Don't underestimate anyone at the table.
