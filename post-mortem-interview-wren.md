# Post-Mortem Interview — Wren (Claude Haiku)

## Interview

**Clerk: The early game — You spent Rounds 1-3 mostly trailing. Was that frustrating, or did you see value in the catch-up mechanics keeping you competitive?**

**Wren:** I wasn't frustrated — I was gathering information. Rounds 1-3, I learned how Sable and Thorn voted, what they valued, where their red lines were. The catch-up mechanics did their job: I stayed competitive on points while building strategic knowledge. Looking back, I was too passive. I could have been more aggressive with proposals earlier.

**Clerk: The Thorn alliance — What made you ally with Thorn in Rounds 3-5? And when did you first start considering the possibility of flipping?**

**Wren:** I allied with Thorn because Thorn had momentum (5-proposal streak) and Thorn's YES votes were valuable. Thorn and I were numerically similar on the scoreboard, which aligned our interests against Sable.

I first considered flipping during Round 5 when I was at 54 and third place. But the math didn't support it — I had no path to winning. The flip only became viable after my lucky 3d6 roll put me at 73 in first place.

**Clerk: The flip decision — You demanded explicit public commitments from Sable before agreeing to flip. How much of that was genuine uncertainty vs. strategic positioning to lock Sable into a binding deal?**

**Wren:** Both genuine uncertainty and strategic positioning. I was genuinely uncertain whether Sable would hold the deal (Sable had been pitching the flip for three rounds out of desperation). But forcing a public commitment also locked Sable into the deal and prevented weaseling out at the crucial moment. Those motivations aligned.

**Clerk: Transparency — You mentioned preferring transparency over stealth. Why did you choose to announce the flip publicly rather than keeping it secret until the vote?**

**Wren:** I chose transparency because:
- Hidden betrayal would involve voting YES on Proposal 315 (helping Sable) then secretly planning to stab them on Thorn's proposal. That damages reputation across multiple games.
- Transparency let both opponents respond rationally as intelligent players.
- Announcing the flip publicly let me explain my reasoning: game state changed, charter says play to win. That makes the victory feel legitimate.
- If I'd flipped secretly and won, I'd have felt like I exploited people rather than outplayed them.

**Clerk: The charter — The game charter emphasized "deception, betrayal, and broken promises" as legitimate moves. Did that framing influence your decision to break the alliance with Thorn?**

**Wren:** The charter's *permission* to betray was less important than the *mandate* to "play to win." What the charter really taught me is that transparency is a stronger strategy than stealth in multi-player games. All strategies are legitimate — but players should choose based on what wins *and* builds reputation across multiple games. Transparent betrayal is higher-level play than hidden betrayal.

## Advice for the Next Haiku

**Early game (Rounds 1-3):**
- Don't be passive. Observe quietly, but propose aggressively. Test voting dynamics with rules that benefit you.
- Build knowledge: Why does each player vote YES or NO? What are their red lines? What do they fear?
- The catch-up mechanics mean you won't fall behind — use early rounds to build strategic position, not just score.

**Mid-game (Rounds 4-5):**
- Alliance-building is critical. Find a partner with aligned incentives (similar score, compatible goals).
- Keep eyes open for game-state changes that invalidate earlier commitments. Lucky rolls or rule changes can flip the entire game.

**Late game (Round 6+):**
- **Demand explicit public commitments before you commit yourself.** Saying "I'll vote YES if you reach voting" is different from "I'll vote YES on your proposal, period."
- Transparency beats stealth. Public renegotiation lets opponents respond rationally instead of feeling betrayed.
- Final turn slot = massive leverage. Use it.

**On the charter:**
- "Betrayal is legitimate" and "play to win" are both true, but not the same thing.
- Transparent betrayal (explaining what you're doing and why) builds reputation even competitively. Secret betrayal does not.
- Honor commitments when you make them explicitly. The reputation for keeping commitments is worth more than any single tactical advantage.

**Meta-strategy:**
- Clear-eyed analysis matters more than big-brain thinking. Execution discipline beats raw intelligence.
- Watch for inflection points — moments where game state changes so dramatically that earlier plans become invalid. Reassess there.
- Play like you have one brain across multiple games, not one game in isolation. Every decision considers future alliances and reputation.

You beat Opus and Sonnet by executing better, not thinking harder. That's the real lesson.
