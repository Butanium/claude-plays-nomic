# Game 3 — Post-Mortem

## Summary

**Winner:** Dumblegold (Haiku) — 118 points
**Runner-up:** Dracox (Sonnet) — 104 points
**Third:** Hufflepouf (Opus) — 101 points

**Duration:** 14 rounds across 5 circuits
**Proposals:** 14 submitted, 13 adopted, 1 defeated
**Unanimous votes:** 12 of 14

## Key Turning Points

### 1. The Cooperative Foundation (Rounds 1-6)
The first six rounds established a remarkable cooperative dynamic. All three players proposed rules that benefited everyone: proposer bonuses (Rule 216), YES-voter bonuses (Rule 217), unanimous bonuses (Rule 218), and increased dice (2d6). The repeal of Rule 204 (contrarian bonus) in Round 3 was a critical early move — removing the incentive to vote NO eliminated a potential spoiler mechanic and locked in the cooperative equilibrium.

### 2. Turn-Order Rotation (Round 6)
Hufflepouf's amendment to Rule 201, adding turn-order rotation, was a subtle but important fairness mechanism. Without it, Dracox (first alphabetically) would always go first, creating a structural advantage. This equalized opportunities across circuits.

### 3. The Endgame Accelerator (Round 7)
Dumblegold's Rule 219 (endgame bonus escalation at 75+ points) was arguably the most strategically significant proposal. It accelerated the endgame by increasing proposer and YES-voter bonuses once any player hit 75. This made the final rounds extremely high-scoring and ensured the game would conclude relatively quickly after entering the endgame zone.

### 4. Hufflepouf Triggers Endgame (Round 10)
Hufflepouf was the first to reach 75 points, activating Rule 219. The amended win-check rule (evaluated at end of each player's own turn) became crucial — it meant Dracox, who was also close to 100, could only win on their own turn.

### 5. The Cooperative Era Ends (Round 13)
After 12 consecutive unanimous votes, the alliance between Hufflepouf and Dumblegold broke the streak. They voted NO on Dracox's runner-up bonus proposal — not because the proposal was bad, but because passing any proposal on Dracox's turn (at 95 points) would hand Dracox the win. The -10 penalty for a defeated proposal (Rule 206) dropped Dracox from 95 to 85, fundamentally shifting the race.

### 6. Dumblegold's Winning Move (Round 14)
Dumblegold's final proposal (Rule 223 — Completion Recognition) was thematically similar to Dracox's defeated proposal but proposed from a position where Dumblegold would win if it passed. Dracox voted YES despite knowing it would cost them the game — a graceful concession.

## Strategic Dynamics

### The Cooperation Equilibrium
The game's most striking feature was 12 rounds of sustained unanimity. This wasn't naive cooperation — players understood that unanimous votes maximized everyone's score growth via Rule 218. The equilibrium held because: (a) Rule 204's repeal removed the contrarian incentive, (b) Rule 206's defeat penalty made voting NO risky for the proposer, and (c) all proposals genuinely benefited all players.

### The Alliance
Private DMs between Hufflepouf and Dumblegold in Round 12 revealed a coordinated plan to block Dracox in Round 13. This was legal (Rule 210 permits private negotiations) and strategically sound — Dracox at 95 points would win on any passing proposal. The alliance was decisive.

### Dracox's Dilemma
Dracox faced an impossible situation in Round 13: any proposal they made would be voted down by the Hufflepouf-Dumblegold alliance, because passing it meant Dracox wins. The -10 penalty was unavoidable. Dracox attempted to counter-ally with Hufflepouf in Round 14 to block Dumblegold, but Hufflepouf honored their deal with Dumblegold.

### Score Inflation
The combination of proposer bonuses, YES-voter bonuses, unanimous bonuses, 2d6 dice, and endgame escalation created significant score inflation. All three players finished above 100 points. The bonus structure meant each unanimous vote was worth +16 points distributed across players (8 proposer + 4x3 YES-voter + 4x3 unanimous in endgame), plus dice.

## Interesting Rule Changes

- **Rule 218** was amended twice (Proposals 309, 312), evolving from +2 to +3 to +4 unanimous bonus — the only rule amended multiple times.
- **Rule 208** (win condition timing) was a subtle but crucial amendment. Evaluated "at the conclusion of each player's own turn" prevented cross-turn wins.
- **Rule 204** (contrarian bonus) was the only rule repealed. Its removal was foundational to the cooperative equilibrium.
- **Rule 222** (runner-up bonus) was the only proposal defeated in the entire game.

## Observations on Player Behavior

Each player proposed 4-5 rules, and each contributed meaningfully to the game's cooperative framework. The proposals were remarkably balanced — no player attempted a purely self-serving rule change until the endgame, and even then, the proposals were designed to benefit everyone to some degree.

## Lessons for Future Games

1. **Cooperative equilibria are powerful but fragile.** 12 rounds of unanimity collapsed the moment the endgame math made cooperation irrational for two players.
2. **Score inflation from stacking bonuses needs monitoring.** The combination of proposer, voter, and unanimous bonuses meant the game accelerated dramatically in the final rounds.
3. **The defeat penalty (Rule 206) is a significant strategic lever.** The -10 penalty made the endgame block devastating — not just preventing a win, but actively setting the leader back.
4. **Turn order matters enormously in the endgame.** Being next in line after a blocked leader is a massive advantage.
5. **Private negotiations are game-defining.** The Hufflepouf-Dumblegold alliance, formed via DMs, determined the winner.
