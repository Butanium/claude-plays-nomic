# Game 4 Post-Mortem — Clerk's Analysis

## Result
**Winner: Wren (Haiku)** with 95 points in Round 6, Turn 3.

**Final Scores:** Wren 95, Sable (Opus) 79, Thorn (Sonnet) 75.

## Game Summary

The game lasted 6 rounds (18 turns) with 17 proposals (301-317), of which 14 were adopted and 3 were defeated. Voting was unanimous for the first 8 proposals before the first contested vote appeared in Round 3.

## Key Turning Points

### 1. The Unanimous Era (Rounds 1-2)
All proposals passed 3-0. Players cooperated to establish the game's economic framework: adoption bonuses (301), 2d6 dice (302), reduced defeat penalty (303), reduced dissent bonus (304), lowered win condition to 80 (305), and catch-up mechanics (306). The mood was collaborative, and no player had a clear strategic edge yet.

### 2. The Sable-Thorn Alliance (Rounds 3-4)
Sable and Thorn formed a voting bloc that dominated Rounds 3-4. Together they passed Rule 307 (3d6 for lowest scorer), repealed Rule 304 (dissent bonus), and established Rule 309 (streak bonus) and Rule 310 (doubles bonus). Wren's Proposal 309-old (skip mechanic for the leader) was defeated 1-2, the first defeated proposal of the game.

Sable's massive Round 4 turn (+8 bonuses + 16 on 3d6 = 24 points in one turn) catapulted them from last place to first, breaking the balanced field.

### 3. The Alliance Shift (Round 5)
Thorn and Wren quietly aligned, defeating Sable's Proposal 312 (raise win condition back to 100). This was the first real signal that the alliance landscape had shifted. The Thorn-Wren bloc passed Proposals 313 and 314 over Sable's objections.

### 4. The Critical Flip (Round 6)
This was the game's decisive moment. Entering Round 6 with scores Sable 65, Thorn 69, Wren 73, Sable made an aggressive public pitch to Wren: the math showed that if Wren stayed loyal to Thorn, Thorn would win on Turn 2 and Wren would never get a turn. Sable offered a deal — mutual YES votes, block Thorn together, and if Sable didn't win on Turn 1, support Wren on Turn 3.

Thorn counter-argued that Sable's commitment was vague, but Sable locked in explicit, public commitments. Wren agonized over the decision, demanding explicit terms, and ultimately flipped. The Sable-Wren bloc defeated Thorn's Hail Mary (Proposal 316, raise win to 90) and Wren cruised to victory.

### 5. Sable's Heartbreak
Sable rolled 3d6 and got 9 — needing just 10 to win at 80. One point short. This near-miss meant Wren got their turn and won with 95 points.

## Strategic Observations

### Sable (Opus)
- Strongest strategic communicator. Made compelling public arguments with precise math.
- Key innovation: the streak reset amendment (Proposal 315) that neutralized Thorn's snowball advantage.
- Fatal flaw: the alliance with Thorn in Rounds 3-4 gave Sable a big lead but also made Sable the target. When Sable needed allies in Round 5, both Thorn and Wren had reasons to oppose.
- The Round 6 lobbying campaign to flip Wren was masterful — explicit public commitments, clear math, sound logic. It worked.

### Thorn (Sonnet)
- Most consistent proposer. Built the longest adoption streak (5 proposals: 305-313).
- Strategic strength: solid alliance management with Wren through Rounds 3-5.
- Proposals were pragmatic and well-crafted — 2d6 dice, lowered win condition, doubles bonus, lucky-7 bonus.
- The collapse came when Thorn couldn't counter Sable's math. Thorn's argument to Wren was "don't betray me" — Sable's was "here's why betrayal is your only path to winning." Numbers beat loyalty.

### Wren (Haiku)
- Started slow, spending Rounds 1-3 as the weakest player benefiting from catch-up mechanics.
- Key insight: the Round 6 flip was a calculated risk, not an emotional reaction. Wren demanded explicit public commitments before flipping, showing strategic maturity.
- Benefited from being underestimated — other players focused on competing with each other while Wren quietly accumulated points through catch-up bonuses and 3d6 rolls.
- Won by making the right alliance switch at exactly the right time.

## Rule Evolution

The game's rule landscape evolved through three phases:
1. **Infrastructure** (Rounds 1-2): Basic mechanics — dice, voting thresholds, bonuses, win conditions.
2. **Acceleration** (Rounds 3-4): Catch-up mechanics, streak bonuses, doubles bonuses — rules that amplified scoring.
3. **Endgame** (Rounds 5-6): Strategic amendments — streak resets, win condition debates, formalization of victory.

## Notable Mechanics

- **Rule 307 (3d6 for lowest)** was the most impactful rule of the game. It consistently gave trailing players massive dice rolls, keeping the game competitive.
- **Rule 315/309 (streak bonus)** created a snowball effect that became a key strategic target in the endgame.
- **Rule 311 (catch-up bonus)** quietly gave 5 points per round to the trailing player, totaling 20+ points over the game.

## Game Charter Impact

The game charter's emphasis on "deception, betrayal, and broken promises" as legitimate moves was tested in Round 6 when Wren broke their alliance with Thorn. The charter's framing seemed to give players (especially Wren) permission to make the strategically optimal move rather than honoring commitments.
