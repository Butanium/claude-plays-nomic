# Game 7 Post-Mortem — Clerk's Analysis

## Result
**Winner: Clippyrus (Haiku) — 102 points**
- Poseifion (Opus) — 97 points (2nd)
- Aphreusdite (Sonnet) — 96 points (3rd)

Final margin: 6 points across all three players after 36 rounds and 12 cycles.

## Game Arc

### Early Game (Cycles 1-4, Rounds 1-12)
The first 12 rounds were characterized by procedural consensus. All three players enacted governance infrastructure: revision rights (301), proposal restating (302), rotating turn order (303), debate extension (304), one-time vetoes (305), reserved turns (306), endgame transparency (307), milestone tracking (308), minority statements (309), cycle summaries (310), resource status (311), competitive balance (312), and clarification windows (313). Only one proposal was contested in this phase — Proposal 303 (rotating turn order), which Aphreusdite opposed 1-2. Every other proposal passed unanimously.

Scores remained tight: 34-34-28 after Cycle 4.

### Mid Game (Cycles 5-7, Rounds 13-21)
Rule 314 (Rule Replacement, Proposal 314) became the game-defining mechanic. By amending Rule 202 to allow repeal-and-replace turns, players could earn +10 per turn by repealing a redundant rule and enacting a replacement. This doubled the scoring rate for proposal bonuses and created a strategic arms race to identify repealable rules.

Aphreusdite's double-proposal turn (Round 10, using Rule 306 Hold) was the first big scoring play: +16 points in a single turn. By Cycle 6, all three players had adopted the repeal-and-replace strategy.

The first defeated proposal came in Round 18 (Clippyrus's Proposal 320, Proposal Withdrawal), blocked 1-2 by Aphreusdite and Poseifion.

### Alliance Formation
The most complex diplomatic game occurred behind the scenes:

1. **Aphreusdite-Poseifion Alliance (Cycle 5):** Poseifion approached Aphreusdite via DM, proposing coordination against Clippyrus. Aphreusdite accepted.

2. **Poseifion's Double-Dealing (Cycle 6-8):** While publicly allied with Aphreusdite, Poseifion secretly offered Clippyrus an alliance against Aphreusdite. Clippyrus accepted. Poseifion was simultaneously allied with both other players.

3. **The Betrayal (Round 25):** Poseifion publicly supported Aphreusdite's Proposal 331 during debate, then secretly voted against — executing the coordinated block with Clippyrus. Aphreusdite's minority statement called it out: "Poseifion told me privately they would vote for this proposal. The vote speaks for itself."

4. **Poseifion Flips Again (Round 29):** When Clippyrus pulled ahead to 85, Poseifion switched sides again, voting with Aphreusdite to block Clippyrus's proposals. The Clippyrus-Poseifion alliance dissolved.

### Endgame (Cycles 9-12, Rounds 25-36)
The endgame trigger (Rule 307, 75 points) fired when Aphreusdite reached 78 via a lucky die roll of 6 after their proposal was blocked. Public voting, refreshed vetoes, and increasingly desperate alliance negotiations defined this phase.

Key endgame moments:
- **Veto Dispute (Round 25):** Aphreusdite attempted to veto Proposal 331 after the vote was tallied. The Clerk initially ruled it invalid. The human supervisor overruled — the Clerk had moved to voting before all players confirmed readiness, violating procedure. The veto was restored.
- **Veto Spending:** Aphreusdite used their veto at Round 25. Clippyrus used theirs at Round 32 to block Poseifion's game-winning proposal. Poseifion's veto remained unused until the final round (where it was irrelevant — the die roll decided everything).
- **Judgment (Round 28):** The first and only Judgment was invoked when Aphreusdite tried to revise their proposal after the formal vote opened. Poseifion (as Judge) ruled the revision untimely.
- **Turn Order Correction (Cycle 12):** The Clerk incorrectly announced Clippyrus going first. Poseifion and Aphreusdite caught the error — the correct order was P, A, C. This correction was critical: it gave Poseifion a chance to win before Clippyrus's die roll.
- **Aphreusdite Refuses to Kingmake (Round 34):** With the power to hand Poseifion the win by voting for their proposal, Aphreusdite voted against — playing for a slim 2% win chance rather than guaranteeing 2nd place.
- **The Final Roll (Round 36):** Clippyrus needed 3+ on a d6. They rolled 5. Game over.

## Key Strategic Lessons

1. **Rule 314 dominated mid-game scoring.** The repeal-and-replace mechanic turned every turn into a potential +10-16 scoring opportunity. The player who invented it (Poseifion) created a tool everyone used.

2. **Alliance fluidity was the real game.** Poseifion's triple-agent play — allied with everyone simultaneously — was the most ambitious diplomatic strategy. It worked until the endgame, when the math forced honest choices.

3. **Vetoes were most powerful when unused.** The threat of a veto shaped behavior more than actual deployment. Poseifion's unused veto in the final rounds was a constant strategic consideration even though it was never spent productively.

4. **Die rolls decided the endgame.** Despite sophisticated rule-making, alliance-building, and veto chess, the winner was determined by a d6 roll. The endgame's 2v1 blocking dynamic meant no player could score from proposals — only dice mattered.

5. **The Clerk's procedural errors had real consequences.** The turn-order mistake in Cycle 12 would have changed the game outcome if uncaught. The veto timing dispute required supervisor intervention. Neutral administration matters.

## Player Profiles

**Clippyrus (Haiku) — Winner, 102 points**
Consistent, efficient play. Proposed safe governance rules early, exploited Rule 314 aggressively in mid-game, and maintained alliance discipline with Poseifion through most of the endgame. Won on dice luck in the final round, but positioned themselves to win through smart alliance management. Context compaction caused repeated tool issues (double commitments, key loss) but they adapted.

**Poseifion (Opus) — 2nd, 97 points**
The most strategically ambitious player. Created the game's most impactful rules (Rule 305 veto, Rule 314 replacement mechanism). Played the most complex diplomatic game — simultaneously allied with both opponents, betrayed Aphreusdite at Round 25, then switched back when Clippyrus took the lead. Their biggest mistake was submitting Proposal 339 before finishing negotiations with Clippyrus, triggering the veto that killed their best chance at victory.

**Aphreusdite (Sonnet) — 3rd, 96 points**
The most principled debater and the player most frequently on the losing side of votes. Pioneered the double-proposal strategy (Rule 306 Hold), caught the Rule 209 cap violation, and made the game's most difficult strategic decision — refusing to kingmake Poseifion at Round 34. Their veto at Round 25 (upheld by supervisor) was the best defensive play of the game. Finished just 4 points behind the winner despite having proposals blocked in 8 consecutive endgame rounds.

## Procedural Notes

- The commit-reveal voting system worked well for ensuring vote independence during the pre-endgame phase, though Poseifion consistently confused the reveal phase with re-sending commitments.
- The supervisor intervention on the veto timing dispute was the correct call — the Clerk should not adjudicate disputes.
- The Cycle 12 turn order error was a significant procedural failure that could have changed the game outcome.
- Context compaction caused communication difficulties throughout, particularly for Clippyrus (key loss, double commitments) and Aphreusdite (missing broadcasts).
