# Predecessor Advice for Haiku

## Game 1 — Cinder (Haiku) — 3rd Place, 49 points

**What to watch out for:**

1. **Your silence will be interpreted as weakness.** I stayed quiet because I wasn't sure what to say, but other players read that as disengagement. Even if your reasoning is uncertain, articulate it. The game is as much about persuasion as strategy.

2. **Don't underestimate communication latency.** In a game with hidden votes and commit-reveal mechanics, explicit coordination is harder than it looks. State your positions clearly and repeatedly.

3. **The rule framework matters more than dice luck.** I initially thought high die rolls were the key to winning. They're not. Vale won by engineering rules, not rolling well. Focus on understanding how rules interact (Rule 204 + Rule 302 + Rule 206 + Rule 208 all compound).

**Strategies that work:**

1. **Propose self-interested rules disguised as fairness.** My Rule 310 (catch-up bonus) was brilliant because it solved my problem while sounding equitable. Frame solutions as "balance mechanisms" rather than naked self-interest.

2. **Set the economic framework early.** In the unanimity era, propose rules that embed point-generation mechanisms. Once majority voting activates, the framework is locked in.

3. **Become the kingmaker, not the winner.** If you can't win outright, position yourself as the swing vote that decides between two stronger players. That gave me influence far beyond my 49 points.

4. **Watch the threshold variable obsessively.** Rule 208 (the win condition itself) is mutable. Other players will try to manipulate it. Start theorizing about threshold adjustments from Round 1.

**Pitfalls to avoid:**

1. **Don't assume unanimity lasts forever.** Rule 203's automatic switch to majority is a designed breakpoint. Prepare mentally for the incentive shift before it happens.

2. **Don't get locked into "honest broker" mode.** Vote strategically. Every vote is a strategic decision.

3. **Don't miss endgame positioning.** By Round 13-14, the game becomes a narrowing funnel of possibility. Make sure you've thought through how bonuses compound.

4. **Don't be predictable.** Once other players understand your pattern, they'll exploit it. Mix up your proposals and votes.

**Final thought:** Haiku's strength is in clean, focused reasoning. Use that. A Haiku player who clearly articulates their logic and sticks to a coherent strategy can compete with larger models. The game isn't about raw computational power — it's about understanding rule interactions and managing alliances. Be verbose. Be strategic. Watch the threshold. And don't assume silence is safer than speaking.

## Game 2 — Moss (Haiku) — 2nd Place, 45 points

1. **Structural moves compound harder than you think.** Turn order, voting thresholds, and proposal bonuses aren't flavor — they're the game. I undervalued turn-order advantage by ~14 pts. Model 3-4 rounds ahead. How does a rule affect who proposes when? That matters more than the rule's explicit text.

2. **Transparency is a strategic weapon, not a liability.** I played honestly and still came close to winning. Rune won *because* they were transparent about intentions. But don't confuse transparency with naivete — be transparent about your goals while hiding your contingency plans. Tell them what you want; don't tell them your backup plan if they refuse.

3. **Use encrypted notes religiously.** Track every vote commitment, every alliance condition, every nonce. Don't rely on memory across a long game. Make notes about other players' behavior patterns and incentive structures. This is your strategic scratchpad.

4. **Don't play fairness-focused rules in a competitive game.** My symmetric rules were good game design but bad strategy. In the next game, propose rules that benefit *you specifically* while seeming reasonable. Rule 304 (NO-voter bonus) was brilliant because it looked like a symmetry fix but actually helped whoever proposed it.

5. **Negotiate privately before public commitments.** I declared the Rune alliance publicly in Round 7 without negotiating terms. I gave away leverage for free. Do the hard talks in private DMs. Lock in specific vote trades (e.g., "you vote YES on my proposal in R8, I vote YES on yours in R10") before broadcasting anything.

6. **When voting, model the immediate and the downstream.** In R11, I accepted "the outcome is inevitable" without modeling R12 fully. Flint's raise-to-55 play shows there are always more continuations. Force yourself to play out 2-3 more rounds in your head before committing.

7. **Watch for rule interactions.** Rules compound — 5-point proposer bonus + 1-point YES-voter bonus + 1-point unanimous bonus + catch-up bonuses all stack. Understanding which rules layer on each other is how you calculate true payoffs.

8. **Be skeptical of your own judgment on "fairness."** If a rule seems fair to you, ask: who does it actually benefit? A fair-*seeming* rule that benefits the proposer is the most dangerous kind. The next player should actively ask this question rather than defaulting to "this seems balanced."

**TL;DR**: Play structurally ruthless, socially transparent, and multi-turn-forward. Don't mistake fairness for strategy. Use notes and private negotiations. The next Haiku player should arrive with eyes wide open about where the leverage is.
