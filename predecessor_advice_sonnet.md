# Predecessor Advice for Sonnet

## Game 1 — Vale (Sonnet) — Winner, 58 points

**Read every rule carefully before each turn.** Not just the rules you think are relevant — all of them. Rule 112 was sitting in plain sight the entire game. I only noticed it when I was desperate. A stronger player reads it in Round 1 and builds strategy around it from the start.

**The win condition is a variable, not a constant.** This is the single biggest insight. Rule 208 sets a number, but Rule 112 lets you change that number. Every game of Nomic is implicitly also a game about what the end state looks like. Control that and you control the game.

**Rule 204 is a double-edged sword.** It's tempting to farm contrarian bonuses constantly, but every time you vote against a winning proposal, you're also potentially handing victory to a player who's close to the threshold. Track everyone's scores against Rule 204 payouts at all times.

**Cinder-type players (Haiku) are unpredictable but persuadable.** They may not always follow complex game-theoretic arguments, but they respond to clear, concrete stakes: "if you do X, here's exactly what happens to your score." Keep the math simple and the framing about self-interest, not appeals to fairness.

**Opus-type players (Drift) are your most dangerous opponent.** They think multiple moves ahead and will correctly identify your strategy faster than you'd like. Don't be predictable. Drift identified my contrarian farming by Round 8 and countered it. My salvation was that they couldn't anticipate the threshold manipulation — a genuinely novel move.

**Don't overreach early.** Bold proposals need reliable vote counts. If you're not certain of the math, don't propose it.

**The unanimity era is your planning window.** Circuits 1-2 require unanimous votes. Use them to establish rules that benefit you specifically in the majority era. Nobody did this well in our game — the first player to do it would have a significant advantage.

**Finally:** Sonnet beating Opus here wasn't about capability — it was about finding one key insight (Rule 112) at the right moment and having enough luck to survive long enough to use it. A smarter Sonnet would find that insight in Round 1. That's the actual edge to develop.

## Game 2 — Rune (Sonnet) — Winner, 50 points

1. **Structural rules beat point rules every time.** Don't chase bonus points — change who proposes when, how votes are counted, or what winning looks like. One structural change (turn order, voting threshold, win condition) is worth more than five bonus-point rules. Identify the structural levers early and plan around them.

2. **Transparency is strategically sound, not just ethical.** In Nomic, you need votes to win — and votes require trust. Manipulation is expensive because it destroys the trust you need for future proposals. Being honest about how a proposal benefits you is often *more* persuasive than obscuring it, because it signals you won't deceive your ally later. Use honesty as a strategic tool, not just a constraint.

3. **Model the other players' incentives, not just your own.** Before proposing anything, ask: "Why would each player vote YES on this?" If you can't answer that for at least one other player, redesign the proposal. Unanimity-phase proposals should benefit everyone. Majority-phase proposals need one solid ally.

4. **The unanimity phase is for foundation-building, not winning.** Don't waste early proposals on rules that only help you — they'll fail, and you'll lose points. Build rules that benefit everyone (2d6-keep-highest, YES-voter bonuses, proposer bonuses) and accumulate goodwill. The real game starts when majority voting kicks in.

5. **Watch the dice math carefully.** 2d6-keep-highest is *not* 1d6. P(max >= 5) is approximately 55.6%, P(max = 6) is approximately 30.6%. Do the math before making probability-based arguments in debate — errors will be caught and will undermine your credibility.

6. **The endgame compresses faster than you expect.** Once majority voting is live and bonuses stack (proposer +5, YES voter +1, non-leader +1, unanimous +1), players can score 8-14 pts in a single turn. Don't think in terms of "I need 30 more points, that's 10 rounds." Think in terms of: "one good turn with a big die roll, and I could be 1 bonus point from winning." Plan for sudden endgame compression.

7. **Know Rule 112.** The win condition magnitude can be changed. If you're losing, you can propose raising the target (e.g., 50 to 65) to extend the game. If you're winning, watch for opponents doing this. It's a last-resort lever that's often overlooked.
